# Passion Engine — Finding the Spike Without Burning the Child

**Goal:** help a student who has cleared the floor (SAT 1570+, 5s on BC Calculus, Physics C, Literature, and Language) find and build a genuine *spike* — the differentiating pursuit that actually gets them looked at — without pressuring them into it, burning them out, or welding them to an identity they picked at eleven.

**One line summary:** A passion is not discovered by a survey; it is built through repeated work, and the only trustworthy sign you've found one is that the child keeps coming back *after* every reward, deadline, and nudge is switched off.

**Thesis.** The floor is table stakes; the spike is the differentiator. But the standard way to "find a passion" is worse than useless — it is actively harmful. "Follow your passion" tells a child their interest is a fixed thing waiting to be uncovered, so the first time the work gets hard they conclude they picked wrong and quit. Interest inventories and "what do you like?" surveys measure a self-report that barely predicts behavior. Gamified engagement — points, streaks, leaderboards — corrupts the very interest it claims to measure, and does so *more* in children than in adults. So we build the opposite of all three. We treat interest as something **developed through work, not discovered by introspection**; we measure it by **unprompted voluntary return** after external pressure is removed, not by what a child says; and we hold every interest as a **mutable hypothesis with evidence and counter-evidence**, never a label. Then a Specialization Planner turns a *validated* interest into an ambitious project spine while keeping the exits open — because the healthy version of a passion is one the child can walk away from.

## DOK 1: Facts and definitions

The vocabulary the argument runs on. Plain factual statements, not yet positions.

- **Interest** — "the psychological state of engaging or the predisposition to reengage with particular classes of objects, events, or ideas over time" (Hidi & Renninger, 2006). Re-engagement, not momentary attention, is the core construct.
- **Four-phase model of interest development** — (1) *triggered situational* → (2) *maintained situational* → (3) *emerging individual* → (4) *well-developed individual* interest. Phases 1–2 need external support; phases 3–4 are self-generated. The boundary is whether re-engagement *requires external support*.
- **Catch vs. hold** (Mitchell, 1993) — it is easy to *catch* situational interest (novelty, puzzles) and hard to *hold* it; hold factors (meaningfulness, personal involvement) predict lasting interest.
- **Intrinsic motivation** — doing an activity for its inherent satisfaction; measured behaviorally by free-choice persistence when alone, unincentivized.
- **Extrinsic motivation / external regulation** — behavior done to satisfy an external demand or contingency; "unlikely to continue when the external contingencies are removed."
- **Self-Determination Theory (SDT)** — three basic needs, *autonomy, competence, relatedness*, support internalized motivation. The **autonomous-vs-controlled** distinction predicts sustained behavior better than intrinsic-vs-extrinsic.
- **Over-justification effect** — giving an *expected* reward for an already-intrinsically-interesting activity reduces later free-choice engagement in it.
- **Free-choice paradigm** — the benchmark behavioral measure of intrinsic motivation: time freely spent on an activity in an unobserved window after incentives are withdrawn.
- **Harmonious passion (HP)** — from *autonomous* internalization; flexible, durable, well-being-promoting; the person can choose to disengage.
- **Obsessive passion (OP)** — from *controlled* internalization (pressure, contingencies, "can't help it"); rigid persistence, burnout, injury, fragile self-worth.
- **Burnout** — physical/mental exhaustion plus a reduced sense of accomplishment leading to devaluation of a once-enjoyable activity; a primary driver of attrition.
- **Fixed vs. growth theory of interest** (O'Keefe, Dweck & Walton, 2018) — *fixed*: interests are inherent and waiting to be found ("find your passion"); *growth*: interests are malleable and developed ("develop your passion").
- **Identity foreclosure** — premature commitment to a single domain/identity ("you are our math kid") that the child must defend and that shatters on first difficulty; a specific hazard for gifted children used to easy early mastery.
- **Grit** (Duckworth) — "perseverance and passion for long-term goals"; two facets, *perseverance of effort* and *consistency of interest*.

## DOK 2: Source anchors

Grouped by the claims that carry the design, with the numbers that make them load-bearing.

**(a) Interest is developed through work, not discovered — and in-the-moment interest predicts return better than what a child says up front.**
- Passions "are developed," starting with a spark and growing "through a process involving repeated engagement, positive experiences and accrued knowledge" (O'Keefe, Dweck & Walton, 2018) [17]. Interest deepens across four phases distinguished by the *durability of the predisposition to reengage*; in later phases external motivators "are not required" (Hidi & Renninger, 2006) [1].
- The predictive punchline: it was *situational interest generated during a course — independent of incoming interest —* that predicted free-choice re-engagement (enrollment/majoring up to 7 semesters later, N=858), **not** pre-existing self-reported interest (Harackiewicz et al., 2008) [5].
- Students can *build* interest by finding personal/utility value in a topic (Hulleman & Harackiewicz, 2009) [6]; meaningful, bounded choice improves motivation and effort (Patall, Cooper & Robinson, 2008) [9].

**(b) External incentives corrupt the interest you're trying to measure — worse in children than adults.**
- Expected, contingent tangible rewards undermine free-choice intrinsic motivation (engagement d = −0.40, completion −0.36, performance −0.28), and are **more detrimental for children than for college students** (Deci, Koestner & Ryan, 1999) [11]. The classic "magic markers" over-justification finding: rewarded preschoolers later drew ~half as much in free play (Lepper, Greene & Nisbett, 1973) [10].
- Imposed deadlines "markedly decreased" interest (Amabile et al., 1976) [13]; controlling surveillance reduces later interest unless believed to be *assistive* (Lepper & Greene, 1975) [14]. Praise helps only when *informational*, not controlling (Henderlong & Lepper, 2002) [15]; trait praise backfires after failure, process praise sustains it (Mueller & Dweck, 1998) [16].
- **Contested, flagged honestly:** Cameron & Pierce (1994) called undermining "a myth"; Deci/Koestner/Ryan (1999, 2001) rebutted on method [12]. The boundary condition matters: only *pre-existing* interest, only with *expected* reward.

**(c) The healthy form of a passion is one you can walk away from.**
- The dualistic model: harmonious passion → positive affect, flow, well-being, flexibility; obsessive passion → conflict, rigid persistence, burnout, injury (Vallerand et al., 2003) [19]. In children, autonomy-supportive parents/teachers predict HP and *negatively* predict OP; over-valuing specialization and heavy self-definition through the activity foster OP (Mageau et al., 2009) [20].
- Burnout is a primary attrition driver and rises with age (AAP, *Pediatrics*, 2024) [22]; specialized adolescents report higher exhaustion (d = −0.34) [25]. Autonomous motivation is protective against dropout (β = −0.17) while controlled motivation predicts it (β = +0.19) [27]. Parental involvement is U-shaped: encouraging is the strongest protective factor (β = −0.39), but neglect *and* over-intensity both raise harm [28]. Interventions work (embedded CBT/mindfulness cut exhaustion d = −0.87) [23].

**(d) Early specialization is not the price of elite achievement.**
- "Predictors of rapid junior performance and of long-term senior performance are opposite"; most elite athletes delay specialization to mid-to-late adolescence (Frontiers, 2023) [24]. Deliberate practice explains only ~4% of performance variance in education and <1% in professions (Macnamara, Hambrick & Oswald, 2014) [33]; the "10,000-hour rule" is a popularization Ericsson disavowed. Grit's higher-order structure is unconfirmed, and its *consistency-of-interest* facet is weak — and developmentally ill-suited to 8–14-year-olds who are still forming interests (Credé, Tynan & Harms, 2017) [34].

**(e) You cannot read interest off a child's face, voice, or gaze — and in EU schools it's now illegal.**
- Emotion "cannot be reliably read from facial configurations… variability is the norm" (Barrett et al., 2019) [45]. Engagement detectors degrade out-of-sample (95.7% train → 76% test). The EU AI Act Article 5(1)(f)/Recital 44 prohibits emotion inference in education for "lack of scientific basis" (in force Feb 2, 2025) [46]; the French CNIL ruled school facial recognition disproportionate under GDPR.

**(f) Measuring interest in children is genuinely hard — triangulate, never trust one number.**
- The Interest Development Scale's "motivation to reengage" factor discriminates interest phases most sharply (Boeder et al., 2021) [4] — direct support that re-engagement is the best marker. But self-report and behavioral measures correlate only ~0.4; self-report is reliable only from ~age 8; social-desirability bias inflates the favorable; **no validated instrument measures "durable passion" in 8–14-year-olds.**

## DOK 3: How the pieces compose

The facts chain into one machine governed by a single rule: **an interest is a mutable hypothesis the child helps author, never a label we assign.**

1. **The Interest Lab manufactures the evidence.** Repeated, varied encounters — 18–24 short probes over 8–12 weeks, across at least six *content domains* and six *work modes* (build, investigate, compose, explain, perform, debug, collaborate) — because interest is *developed*, not surveyed, and because a preferred work mode is not a preferred topic. Each probe family has equivalent variants so repeated exposure tests interest, not answer-recall. The child chooses among two or three eligible probes at every choice point (bounded choice, per Patall).

2. **"Return" is the signal, and it is defined against pressure.** The central measurement is *voluntary, unprompted re-engagement* after novelty, praise, deadlines, rewards, reminders, and parent nudges have faded — measured at 7 and 30 days. A return triggered by a reminder or reward carries its intervention context and is **not** counted as a passion signal. This is the Harackiewicz/Boeder finding made operational: re-engagement, not self-report, is the phase marker.

3. **The interest lives as a mutable `InterestHypothesis`.** A versioned evidence record, not a scalar score, with a bitemporal view so past decisions stay replayable. It carries `candidate_domain` (broad, never a career), `work_mode_profile`, a `state` (`EXPLORING` → `EMERGING` → `CANDIDATE_SPINE` → `ACTIVE` → `CONTESTED` → `PARKED` → `REOPENED`), `signal_summary` (return, scope authorship, competence growth, novelty decay, failure recovery, prompt dependence — as *separate* values), explicit `competing_explanations` (novelty, ease, praise, peer belonging, parent pressure, resource access, work-mode-over-topic), `coverage_gaps`, `uncertainty`, a `next_probe`, and a `child_position` (`AGREE`/`UNSURE`/`DISAGREE`/`DECLINE_TO_LABEL`/`REQUEST_TO_PARK`). The lifecycle *permits contradiction*: evidence can lower confidence, split a hypothesis, park a spine, or reopen an old one. It shows the strongest disconfirming evidence next to the strongest supporting evidence, and it never says "you are an audio person" — only "current evidence suggests" and "next test."

4. **Guardrails against the corruption modes.** No expected contingent reward is the score (Deci/Koestner/Ryan); celebration attaches to *process*, not trait (Mueller/Dweck); pressure-bearing events (deadlines, rivalry, nudges) are metered, not maximized; and interest is never inferred from face, voice, or gaze (Barrett; EU AI Act). Missing data cannot be read as low interest until access, health, schedule, equipment, and consent are ruled out.

5. **The Specialization Planner turns a validated interest into a spine — with the exits kept open.** Once a hypothesis reaches `CANDIDATE_SPINE` (≥3 signal families, including one delayed discretionary and one competence signal), the planner allocates a project block: age-tiered splits of primary spine / adjacent depth / wildcard (50/30/20 at 6–8 → 60/25/15 at 9–11 → 70/20/10 at 12–14). The wildcard floor is a permanent, protected route back out. Every plan names exit conditions, disconfirming evidence, and a child-authored question — and "the plan makes no career claim."

6. **Humans decide; the model only advises.** During beta, every model-proposed state change goes to shadow logs and a human guide authors the operative revision. The child co-authors and can decline the label, refuse pressure, or ask to park at any time. A parent gives consent and access context but "cannot convert a parent report into confirmed interest" and cannot override the child's refusal.

## DOK 4: The spiky points of view

Each is false to current consensus and true anyway.

### SPOV 1 — "Find your passion" is harmful advice

The entire culture, and every interest-inventory product, tells a child their passion is a fixed thing waiting to be uncovered. That framing is not just wrong, it is destructive: a fixed theory of interest dampens openness to other domains and *collapses resilience* — "interest flagged significantly more" for people induced to hold a fixed rather than growth theory, because difficulty gets read as proof "it was not their interest after all" (O'Keefe, Dweck & Walton, 2018). Passions are *built* through repeated engagement and accrued knowledge. We teach "develop your passion" and design for the four-phase climb, never the soulmate search.
**Consensus it breaks:** every child has a latent passion to be identified by introspection or a survey.
**Backing:** O'Keefe, Dweck & Walton (2018); Hidi & Renninger (2006); Harackiewicz et al. (2008).

### SPOV 2 — What a child says they love barely predicts what they'll return to

Interest inventories and "which subject do you like best?" are the standard intake, and they measure a self-report that correlates only ~0.4 with behavior. The signal that actually predicts the future is *unprompted voluntary re-engagement after the pressure is gone* — in-the-moment interest, not incoming interest, predicted course choice seven semesters out (Harackiewicz 2008), and "motivation to reengage" is the sharpest phase discriminator (Boeder 2021). So we build the whole measurement around return-after-pressure-removal, and we treat a five-star rating as nearly worthless.
**Consensus it breaks:** ask the kid (or the parent) what they're interested in and build on that.
**Backing:** Harackiewicz et al. (2008); Boeder et al. (2021); Ryan & Deci (2000).

### SPOV 3 — Gamifying engagement corrupts the interest you're trying to measure — and it's worse for kids

Points, streaks, badges, and leaderboards are ed-tech's motivation playbook. They are an over-justification machine: expected contingent tangible rewards undermine free-choice intrinsic motivation (d = −0.28 to −0.40) and are *more detrimental for children than adults* (Deci/Koestner/Ryan 1999); imposed deadlines and controlling surveillance cut interest too. A reward-heavy passion engine literally converts intrinsic interest into external regulation — and then measures the external regulation and calls it passion. We refuse the reward-as-score design and meter every pressure event instead of maximizing it.
**Consensus it breaks:** gamification is a motivation best practice.
**Backing:** Deci, Koestner & Ryan (1999); Lepper, Greene & Nisbett (1973); Amabile et al. (1976).

### SPOV 4 — Early specialization is not the price of a spike

The hothouse assumption is that a decade's head start in one field is how you produce excellence. The data says the traits that produce fast junior success are *opposite* to those that produce senior success; most elites specialize late; deliberate practice explains ~4% of variance in education; and early specialization raises exhaustion and burnout. So we front-load *breadth of exposure* (the Interest Lab), specialize only on validated return evidence, and keep a permanent wildcard floor — a spike is a direction we commit to on evidence, not a cage we lock at eleven.
**Consensus it breaks:** specialize as early and as hard as possible.
**Backing:** Frontiers late-specialization review (2023); Macnamara et al. (2014); AAP (2024).

### SPOV 5 — A passion should never become an identity; the healthy one is the one you can quit

We celebrate the totally-committed prodigy — "she's our math kid" — and grit even demands "consistency of interest." But that is the recipe for obsessive passion: controlled internalization, rigid persistence, burnout, and a self-worth that shatters on the first hard failure (Vallerand 2003; Mageau 2009). And grit's consistency-of-interest facet is weak and developmentally wrong for exploring 8–14-year-olds (Credé 2017). We design for *harmonious* passion: the child can rest, keep other relationships, change direction, and value the work without making continuation a condition of worth. The hypothesis "supports planning; it does not define the child."
**Consensus it breaks:** total, identity-defining commitment is the mark of a serious talent.
**Backing:** Vallerand et al. (2003); Mageau et al. (2009); Credé, Tynan & Harms (2017); O'Keefe et al. (2018).

### SPOV 6 — You cannot read a child's passion off their face

The AI-in-education wave sells "engagement detection," attention tracking, and affect recognition. It is pseudoscience: emotion "cannot be reliably read from facial configurations" (Barrett 2019), detectors collapse out-of-sample, and the EU banned emotion inference in schools for "lack of scientific basis" (AI Act Art. 5, Feb 2025). We infer interest only from what a child *voluntarily does* — returns, revisions, chosen challenge, recovery after failure — never from a webcam.
**Consensus it breaks:** affective computing can measure student engagement.
**Backing:** Barrett et al. (2019); EU AI Act Article 5(1)(f); CNIL rulings.

### SPOV 7 — A preferred *work mode* is not a preferred *topic*

Interest taxonomies are topic-first ("likes science"). But a child may love *debugging* across audio, robotics, and games, or love *explaining* across science and history — the work mode is the durable preference and the topic is the vehicle. (And note: expressed mode preference is not an aptitude claim — the learning-styles "meshing" hypothesis is unsupported, Pashler 2008.) So the Interest Lab varies work modes as a first-class axis, and the hypothesis records a `work_mode_profile` next to the domain.
**Consensus it breaks:** interest is a subject you like.
**Backing:** Hidi & Renninger (2006); Pashler et al. (2008); PRD §4.4.

## People we read, and why

- **Suzanne Hidi & K. Ann Renninger.** The four-phase model — interest as a developed predisposition to *reengage* — is the spine of the whole product and of SPOV 1/2/7.
- **Judith Harackiewicz.** Showed in-the-moment interest beats incoming self-report at predicting real re-engagement years later; the empirical basis for measuring return, not surveys.
- **Edward Deci & Richard Ryan.** Self-Determination Theory (autonomy/competence/relatedness) and the reward-undermining meta-analysis behind SPOV 3 and the whole anti-gamification posture.
- **Paul O'Keefe, Carol Dweck & Gregory Walton.** "Develop your passion, don't find it" — the direct refutation of the cultural default (SPOV 1) and the mindset/process-praise design.
- **Robert Vallerand & Geneviève Mageau.** The harmonious-vs-obsessive passion model and its child/adolescent evidence — the foundation of SPOV 5 and the burnout guardrails.
- **Marcus Credé.** The grit critique — consistency-of-interest is weak and wrong for this age band.
- **Lisa Feldman Barrett.** The evidence that emotion can't be read from the face — SPOV 6.

## Build plan (broad strokes)

Ordered so we build the evidence-generating core first and the intensity/allocation machinery only once the signal is trustworthy.

1. **The Interest Lab.** A probe library indexed by *domain × work mode*, with equivalent variants per family, bounded 2–3 choice at each point, and a balanced 18–24-probe / 8–12-week arc. Offers respect a permanent exploration floor; any adaptive selection stays inside rules and (in beta) shadow-only.
2. **The return-signal event model.** Capture delayed voluntary return (7/30 days), unrequired revision, chosen challenge, recovery after failure, self-authored scope, and prompt dependence — with prompted vs. discretionary events kept strictly distinct. Infer interest only from voluntary action; never from face/voice/gaze.
3. **The mutable `InterestHypothesis` service.** Versioned, bitemporal, contradiction-permitting; stores competing explanations, coverage gaps, uncertainty, and the child's position; shows disconfirming next to supporting evidence; decays stale certainty. Requires ≥3 signal families (incl. one delayed-discretionary + one competence) before `CANDIDATE_SPINE`.
4. **The anti-corruption reward/pressure layer.** Process-praise not trait-praise; celebration on independent effort and productive struggle, never on speed/rank/streaks; every pressure-bearing event (deadline, rivalry, nudge) metered by a short-lived dose token rather than maximized. No dark patterns, no loss-framed streaks.
5. **The Specialization Planner.** Age-tiered spine/adjacent/wildcard allocation with a protected wildcard floor, a project ladder at productive difficulty, and a persistence-and-repair protocol (change one context at a time; park after sustained low voluntary return). Every plan carries exit conditions and a child-authored question; child assent gates any allocation change.
6. **Measurement, honestly.** Triangulate self-report (validated instruments from ~age 8) with behavioral return; treat ~0.4 self-report/behavior convergence as a reason to never trust one number; run a child-comprehension study before scale because no instrument validates "durable passion" at 8–14.

**Our perspective, and what we still don't know.** We're confident the core inversion is right: build interest through work, measure it by return-after-pressure-removal, hold it as a revisable hypothesis, and specialize late and reversibly. That directly contradicts the "find your passion / gamify engagement / specialize early" default, and the evidence is on our side. The honest gaps we will not paper over: (1) the strongest studies (implicit theories of interest, much of the burnout literature) come from college students and adolescents 13–21, so applying them to 6–14 is *reasoned extrapolation, not demonstrated* — it needs its own validation; (2) no validated instrument measures durable passion in this age band, and self-report/behavior converge only weakly, so our measurement has to triangulate and stay humble; and (3) several planks are genuinely contested (reward-undermining boundary conditions, the HP/OP binary, the specialization→burnout pathway), so we design to the *mechanism* — controlled vs. autonomous motivation — rather than to any single contested effect size.
