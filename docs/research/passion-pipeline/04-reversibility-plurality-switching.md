# 04 · Reversibility, Plurality & Switching: The Evidence-Based Policy for a Child's Spike (Ages 6–14)

> Brainlift-style research memo. Operationalizes the parent doc's SPOV 3 ("start early, but keep the spike reversible and plural") into a concrete policy with a switch-vs-quit decision rule. Companion to `../passionBrainlift.md`.

## Thesis

**Reversibility and plurality are not safety nets bolted onto an early start — they are the mechanism that makes an early start work: keeping the exit open and holding 2–3 candidate spikes is what converts precocious depth into durable (harmonious) passion instead of foreclosed, obsessive burnout. The real developmental hazard is foreclosure, not switching; the switching danger (sunk cost) is largely installed by adults and grows with age, so the policy tightens exploration requirements and loosens "don't quit" pressure exactly as the child gets older.**

Scope note on age validity: the strongest *in-band (6–14)* evidence is the child sunk-cost work (ages 4–7), the music sensitive period (before age 7), and the sport sampling model (6–16). The passion, grit, identity-status, and over-scheduling findings skew adolescent/adult and are marked **[extrapolated]** where used for ages 6–10.

## DOK 4 — Spiky Points of View

Five POVs that break with a live consensus and hold up under the evidence.

### SPOV 1 — Reversibility is not a hedge; it is the active ingredient that produces durable passion.

- **Elaboration.** The intuitive model says commitment devices build discipline: take away the exit and the child learns to push through. The evidence says the opposite for *internalized* passion. What separates a harmonious passion (flexible, positive, sustained) from an obsessive one (rigid, conflictual, burnout-prone) is *how* the activity was internalized — autonomously vs. under control (Vallerand et al., 2003). And the developmental antecedents are now measured: in children and teenagers, autonomy support predicts harmonious passion, while over-valuing specialization, fusing the activity into self-definition, and heavy parental valuation predict *obsessive* passion (Mageau et al., 2009). A credible, exercised right to quit is autonomy support made concrete. Remove it and you don't get grit; you get the controlled internalization that predicts the bad outcome.
- **Consensus it breaks.** "Don't let them quit — a no-exit rule builds character and grit."
- **Backing.** Vallerand et al. (2003) 10.1037/0022-3514.85.4.756; Mageau et al. (2009) 10.1111/j.1467-6494.2009.00559.x; Coakley (1992) 10.1123/ssj.9.3.271 (burnout as loss of control + foreclosure); O'Keefe, Dweck & Walton (2018) 10.1177/0956797618780643. **Honest limit:** passion-model evidence is adolescent/adult-weighted **[extrapolated for 6–10]**; "freedom to quit increases staying" is a mechanism claim, and autonomy support ≠ zero structure — reversibility without re-engagement scaffolding degrades into drift (see policy).

### SPOV 2 — The sunk-cost trap belongs to the adults, not the child — and it grows during 6–14. So "wasted investment" is never a valid reason to keep a kid in a spike.

- **Elaboration.** The reflex to prevent switching assumes the child is an impulsive quitter who will regret abandoning what they've "put so much into." But young children are strikingly *immune* to the sunk-cost fallacy: 5–6-year-olds don't anticipate that prior cost should drive choice and respond at chance where adults show the bias (Sehl, Friedman & Denison, 2021); across ages 4–7 only adults, not children, keep the high-cost item (Sehl, Denison & Friedman, 2024); the bias appears only around age 6 and only when children are explicitly prompted to weigh effort, and is "not fundamental to conceptions of choice" — it is a learned, enculturated shift (Sehl, Denison & Friedman, 2026). Humans acquire this bias with age; children and non-human animals are *less* prone than adults (Arkes & Ayton, 1999). Two consequences flip the usual worry. (a) A 6–8-year-old's switch is almost never sunk-cost-irrational, so early switching is genuinely cheap. (b) The escalation-of-commitment trap is something the environment *installs* as the child ages — via "don't be a quitter," accumulated identity, and parental valuation — so the program must guard against manufacturing it, hardest in the 12–14 band.
- **Consensus it breaks.** "Kids under-persist; adults must hold them to prior commitments so effort isn't wasted."
- **Backing.** Sehl, Friedman & Denison (2021) 10.1111/cogs.13063; Sehl, Denison & Friedman (2024) 10.1016/j.cognition.2024.105774; Sehl, Denison & Friedman (2026) 10.1037/xge0001933; Arkes & Ayton (1999) 10.1037/0033-2909.125.5.591. **Honest limit:** direct data covers ages 4–7 + adults; the *monotonic* "grows to 14" is interpolation from the developmental trend, not a measured 8–14 curve. Direction (learned, increasing) is robust; the exact slope is not.

### SPOV 3 — Multipotentiality is mostly a measurement artifact, so "plural" is a search strategy for the child's real tilt — not a belief that they're equally good at everything. Cap it at ~2–3, because breadth pays on an inverted-U.

- **Elaboration.** The counseling folklore that gifted kids are "good at everything" (and therefore paralyzed by choice) does not survive proper measurement. When over 1,000 top-1% adolescents were assessed *above level*, fewer than 5% had the flat ability/interest profile that defines multipotentiality; >95% showed a markedly differentiated profile once the test ceiling was lifted — "it was never there and already it's vanishing" (Achter, Lubinski & Benbow, 1996; 1997). The supposed multipotentiality→career-indecision link is largely an artifact of age-calibrated tools that top out. So most children *have* a strongest domain to find — which justifies searching (plurality) while denying equipotence. But plurality is not free breadth: activity involvement helps on an *inverted-U* — breadth and intensity predict better outcomes up to a point, then decline at high levels (Fredricks, 2012; Busseri et al., 2006), and because far transfer largely fails (Sala & Gobet, 2017), each spike is a separate time cost with no cross-subsidy. Hence a hard ceiling of ~3, narrowing with age.
- **Consensus it breaks.** Either "keep all options open forever, gifted kids shouldn't narrow" **or** the opposite "more activities are always more enrichment."
- **Backing.** Achter, Lubinski & Benbow (1996) 10.1037/0022-0167.43.1.65; Achter, Lubinski & Benbow (1997) 10.1177/001698629704100102; Fredricks (2012) 10.1007/s10964-011-9704-0; Busseri et al. (2006) 10.1037/0012-1649.42.6.1313; Sala & Gobet (2017) 10.1177/0963721417712760. **Honest limit:** SMPY samples are gifted and above-level-tested at ~12–13; resolving a differentiated tilt needs the right instrument, which we largely *lack* for a 6-year-old — so early plurality is justified by our measurement uncertainty, not by proven equipotence. Over-scheduling data is adolescent-weighted **[extrapolated for 6–9]**.

### SPOV 4 — "Consistency of interest" is the wrong virtue to install in a child. Perseverance-of-effort is the one that pays — and that distinction *is* the switch-vs-quit rule.

- **Elaboration.** Grit is sold as sticking to one passion for years and never swerving. But the meta-analytic anatomy of grit shows its two facets are not equal: across 88 samples and 66,807 people, grit's higher-order structure was not confirmed, grit was nearly redundant with conscientiousness (ρ ≈ .84), and *perseverance of effort* carried essentially all the predictive weight while *consistency of interest* added little (Credé, Tynan & Harms, 2017). Even the original grit paper explained only ~4% of outcome variance and defined the consistency facet by items like "My interests change from year to year" scored as a *deficit* (Duckworth et al., 2007). Translation for children: demanding domain loyalty ("stay in this lane") is unsupported; demanding effort *within whatever attempt is live* is supported. So the healthy-switch rule taxes within-attempt effort, never domain constancy.
- **Consensus it breaks.** "Grit means picking one thing and refusing to swerve; teach kids not to change interests."
- **Backing.** Credé, Tynan & Harms (2017) 10.1037/pspp0000102; Duckworth et al. (2007) 10.1037/0022-3514.92.6.1087; O'Keefe, Dweck & Walton (2018) 10.1177/0956797618780643. **Honest limit:** grit evidence is adolescent/adult **[extrapolated for 6–10]**; this does *not* license infinite switching — perseverance still matters, which is exactly why the rule requires clearing one real difficulty before a switch is honored.

### SPOV 5 — Foreclosure, not switching, is the real hazard of an early start — and a designed spike can *manufacture* it unless exploration is structurally required.

- **Elaboration.** A child who "knows exactly what they are" at 8 looks like a success and may be a warning sign. Identity foreclosure — commitment without exploration — is a real, measurable status associated with authoritarian values, external locus, rigidity, and brittle self-esteem under challenge (Marcia, 1966). Foreclosure and diffusion decline across adolescence *as exploration happens*, but exploration is not automatic: a large share never reach identity achievement by young adulthood, and status often stays stable across waves (Kroger, Martinussen & Marcia, 2010). In youth sport, the mechanism is explicit: a unidimensional, sport-fused identity plus loss of control is the pathway to burnout, i.e., foreclosure-driven burnout, not stress-driven (Coakley, 1992). An early, deep, single spike is precisely the setup for a foreclosed identity — unless the program *forces* the exploration that doesn't occur on its own.
- **Consensus it breaks.** "Early certainty and depth are maturity; a kid who's all-in young is ahead."
- **Backing.** Marcia (1966) 10.1037/h0023281; Kroger, Martinussen & Marcia (2010) 10.1016/j.adolescence.2009.11.002; Coakley (1992) 10.1123/ssj.9.3.271. **Honest limit:** identity-status research is adolescent (≈12+); for ages 6–10, "foreclosure" is premature as a *measured* construct — the risk there is prospective (building the scaffolding of a foreclosed identity), not a diagnosis at 8. And Kroger's "developmental foreclosure" can be a benign launchpad, so early commitment is not automatically pathological — only *unexplored, un-exitable* commitment is.

## DOK 3 — Insights (evidence → policy bridge)

- **I1 — Reversibility is causal to durability, not opposed to it.** Autonomy support (of which a real exit is the concrete form) predicts *harmonious* passion; controlled internalization predicts obsessive passion and burnout (Vallerand 2003; Mageau 2009). Therefore the exit is a feature to advertise, not a leak to plug. (→ SPOV 1)
- **I2 — The program, not the child, is the sunk-cost risk.** Because the fallacy is near-absent early and enculturated later (Sehl 2021/2024/2026; Arkes & Ayton 1999), any "you've invested too much to stop" logic is the *adult's* bias projected onto a child who doesn't hold it. Policy must ban prior-investment as a reason to retain, and must protect cool-state decisions as the child ages into the bias. (→ SPOV 2)
- **I3 — Switching cost is a resource-competition fact, not a psychological one, once you cap it.** Far transfer fails (Sala & Gobet 2017), so three spikes cost roughly three times the protected depth-hours — dilution is real if too many spikes chase the same hours. The plurality cap exists to keep at least one spike above the re-engagement threshold, not because switching harms the child. (→ SPOV 3)
- **I4 — Most kids have a real tilt; our instruments just can't see it yet at 6.** >95% of gifted youth show differentiated profiles above-level (Achter 1996/1997), so plurality is a *search* that should converge, not a permanent posture. Early breadth is a hedge against *our* measurement error; as tilt resolves (better around 9–13), narrow. (→ SPOV 3)
- **I5 — Split "passion" into loyalty vs. effort, and only demand effort.** Consistency-of-interest is the weak facet; perseverance-of-effort is the load-bearing one (Credé 2017). The switch rule should therefore gate on "did you push through a real obstacle?" (effort) and be indifferent to "did you stay in the same domain?" (loyalty). (→ SPOV 4)
- **I6 — Fixed-theory language turns a hard patch into a verdict; that is the exact quit-signal to catch.** A fixed theory of interest makes difficulty read as "not my thing" and collapses engagement (O'Keefe 2018). So the *reason a child gives* is diagnostic: identity verdicts ("I'm not a math person") flag avoidance; approach statements ("I keep choosing music") flag a healthy switch. (→ SPOV 4/5)
- **I7 — Exploration must be a scheduled requirement, because it doesn't happen by itself.** Foreclosure persists absent a trigger to explore (Kroger 2010; Marcia 1966), and early single-domain fusion is the burnout pathway (Coakley 1992). So the program *mandates* periodic re-choice checkpoints and a protected second pursuit as anti-foreclosure insurance — most needed at 12–14 when identity work and sunk-cost bias both peak. (→ SPOV 5)
- **I8 — Age flips the dial: loosen "don't quit," tighten "must explore," as the child gets older.** Young children can switch nearly freely (no sunk cost, pre-identity) and should sample widely; older children need a light friction on impulsive exits (real investment now exists) *and* a hard exploration mandate (foreclosure now possible). The two knobs move in opposite directions across 6→14. (→ all)
- **I9 — "Park," not "quit," dissolves the framing problem.** Defaulting a dropped spike to reversible *parked* status (returnable, not deleted) removes the sunk-cost/identity charge, keeps the profile plural, and makes the exit psychologically cheap — the operational form of reversibility. (→ SPOV 1/2)
- **I10 — Domain-timing exceptions are narrow but real.** Motor/rhythm-based domains have a genuine sensitive period before ~age 7 (Watanabe 2007; Steele 2013), and early-peak sports (e.g., gymnastics, figure skating) reward earlier specialization (Côté). So for those specific candidates, protect a *minimum early dosage* even amid free switching — the exception is about *start dosage*, never about locking the child in. (→ SPOV 1/3)

## DOK 2 — Knowledge Tree

DOK-1 = facts as stated by the source; DOK-2 = our summary.

### Category A — Passion, reversibility & burnout

- **Vallerand et al. (2003), "Les Passions de l'Âme: On Obsessive and Harmonious Passion," JPSP 85(4), 756–767.**
  - DOK 1: Harmonious passion arises from *autonomous* internalization (flexible engagement, positive affect); obsessive passion from *controlled* internalization (rigid persistence, conflict, negative affect). Both predict deliberate practice; only OP predicts ill-being.
  - DOK 2: Health depends on *how* a pursuit is internalized, not its intensity. A real right to disengage is autonomy support; removing it pushes toward the obsessive profile.
  - Link: https://doi.org/10.1037/0022-3514.85.4.756
- **Mageau, Vallerand et al. (2009), "On the Development of Harmonious and Obsessive Passion," Journal of Personality 77(3), 601–646.**
  - DOK 1: Across beginners→experts, incl. children/teens: autonomy support predicts *harmonious* passion; valuing activity specialization, self-definition through the activity, and high parental valuation predict *obsessive* passion.
  - DOK 2: The developmental levers are named and are in-band. Over-specialization + identity fusion + parental over-valuation is the obsessive recipe — the exact things plurality and reversibility counter.
  - Link: https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1467-6494.2009.00559.x
- **Coakley (1992), "Burnout among Adolescent Athletes: A Personal Failure or Social Problem?" Sociology of Sport Journal 9(3), 271–285.**
  - DOK 1: Interviews with 15 burned-out adolescent athletes; burnout rooted in social organization that builds a *unidimensional identity* and denies athletes *control* over their lives → identity foreclosure. Remedy = restore control + broaden identity.
  - DOK 2: Burnout here is foreclosure- and control-driven, not stress-driven. Reversibility (control) + plurality (multidimensional identity) are the direct antidotes.
  - Link: https://doi.org/10.1123/ssj.9.3.271

### Category B — Identity development & foreclosure

- **Marcia (1966), "Development and Validation of Ego Identity Status," JPSP 3(5), 551–558.**
  - DOK 1: Four statuses by exploration × commitment: achievement, moratorium, foreclosure, diffusion. Foreclosure = commitment *without* exploration (values adopted from parents/authority); foreclosures scored highest on authoritarianism, set unrealistic goals, and had the most vulnerable self-esteem under challenge.
  - DOK 2: Commitment without exploration is a measurable, costly status. An early spike adopted from adults, never explored, is textbook foreclosure.
  - Link: https://doi.org/10.1037/h0023281
- **Kroger, Martinussen & Marcia (2010), "Identity status change during adolescence and young adulthood: A meta-analysis," Journal of Adolescence 33(5), 683–698.**
  - DOK 1: 124 studies. Longitudinal: progressive change .36, regressive .15, stable .49. Foreclosure & diffusion decline across high school; large proportions are *not* identity-achieved by young adulthood. Distinguishes "developmental" (transitional, benign) vs "firm" foreclosure.
  - DOK 2: Exploration → achievement is common but *far from guaranteed and often doesn't happen*; ~half stay put between waves. Exploration must be engineered, not assumed. Early commitment isn't automatically bad (developmental foreclosure), but *unexplored, unexited* commitment is.
  - Link: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.adolescence.2009.11.002

### Category C — Sunk cost / switching cost in children

- **Sehl, Friedman & Denison (2021), "Blind to Bias? Young Children Do Not Anticipate that Sunk Costs Lead to Irrational Choices," Cognitive Science 45(11), e13063.**
  - DOK 1: 5–6-year-olds (N=185) vs adults (N=117). Adults predict an agent keeps the high-cost object; children respond at chance — first-person and interpersonal. Children *do* track future costs (prefer low-cost to acquire).
  - DOK 2: The sunk-cost reflex is absent in early childhood; children don't treat past cost as "wasted."
  - Link: https://doi.org/10.1111/cogs.13063
- **Sehl, Denison & Friedman (2024), "Emotions before actions: When children see costs as causal," Cognition 245, 105774.**
  - DOK 1: Ages 4–7 (N=320) + adults (N=429). At all ages, participants expect characters to feel *sadder* about losing a high-cost object; only adults predict they'd *keep* it.
  - DOK 2: Children grasp that cost matters emotionally but don't yet let it irrationally drive action — the bias is an incomplete "mental accounting," acquired later.
  - Link: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cognition.2024.105774
- **Sehl, Denison & Friedman (2026), "Why young children think actions aren't swayed by sunk costs: Testing two accounts," JEP: General.**
  - DOK 1: N=484, ages 4–7. Prompted about effort, children ~6+ (not younger) make sunk-cost predictions; even older kids aren't at ceiling. "Sunk costs are not fundamental to conceptions of choice"; the shift reflects broadening of what children see as choice-relevant (firsthand cost experience, enculturation).
  - DOK 2: The bias is *learned* and emerges through childhood — it can be taught in, and by implication, avoided.
  - Link: https://doi.org/10.1037/xge0001933
- **Arkes & Ayton (1999), "The sunk cost and Concorde effects: Are humans less rational than lower animals?" Psychological Bulletin 125(5), 591–600.**
  - DOK 1: Reviews evidence that the sunk-cost effect is largely *absent* in young children and non-human animals and *increases with age* in humans; attributed to overgeneralizing a "don't waste" rule.
  - DOK 2: Escalation-of-commitment is a culturally acquired adult tendency, not a childhood default. (Classic; DOI standard for the record.)
  - Link: https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-2909.125.5.591

### Category D — Multipotentiality (artifact) & how many pursuits

- **Achter, Lubinski & Benbow (1996), "Multipotentiality among the intellectually gifted: 'It was never there and already it's vanishing,'" Journal of Counseling Psychology 43(1), 65–76.**
  - DOK 1: >1,000 top-1% adolescents, above-level SAT + Study of Values + Holland interests. <5% had flat (multipotential) profiles. The multipotentiality→"career indecision and distress" story is largely an artifact of age-calibrated tools with insufficient ceilings.
  - DOK 2: Most gifted kids have a genuinely differentiated strongest domain; "equal potential everywhere" is a ceiling illusion. Plurality is a *search* toward a real tilt, not proof of equipotence.
  - Link: https://doi.org/10.1037/0022-0167.43.1.65
- **Achter, Lubinski & Benbow (1997), "Rethinking Multipotentiality Among the Intellectually Gifted: A Critical Review and Recommendations," Gifted Child Quarterly 41(1).**
  - DOK 1: >95% of gifted adolescents show markedly differentiated ability/interest/value profiles when assessed above-level and all factors are consulted.
  - DOK 2: Confirms the artifact interpretation; above-level assessment resolves the tilt that grade-level tests hide.
  - Link: https://doi.org/10.1177/001698629704100102
- **Fredricks (2012), "Extracurricular Participation and Academic Outcomes: Testing the Over-Scheduling Hypothesis," Journal of Youth and Adolescence 41(3), 295–306.**
  - DOK 1: N=13,130. 10th graders averaged 2–3 activities (~5 h/wk). Breadth *and* intensity positively predicted math scores, grades, expectations, later educational status — but a significant *nonlinear* term meant adjustment *declined* at the highest breadth/intensity.
  - DOK 2: Involvement helps on an inverted-U; there is a real ceiling beyond which more activities/hours hurt. Supports a small, capped number of concurrent pursuits. **[adolescent sample; extrapolated for 6–9]**
  - Link: https://doi.org/10.1007/s10964-011-9704-0
- **Busseri, Rose-Krasnor, Willoughby & Chalmers (2006), "A longitudinal examination of breadth and intensity of youth activity involvement and successful development," Developmental Psychology 42(6), 1313–1326.**
  - DOK 1: Breadth and intensity of involvement relate to positive development, with diminishing/curvilinear returns at high involvement.
  - DOK 2: Independent corroboration of the inverted-U; breadth is good up to a threshold. **[adolescent]**
  - Link: https://doi.org/10.1037/0012-1649.42.6.1313

### Category E — Early vs late specialization; sensitive periods

- **Côté (1999), "The influence of the family in the development of talent in sport," The Sport Psychologist 13(4), 395–417 (Developmental Model of Sport Participation).**
  - DOK 1: Three phases — sampling years (6–12: diversify + deliberate play), specializing years (13–15), investment years (16+). Sampling does not hinder elite outcomes in late-maturation sports and is linked to longer careers and better youth development; early-peak sports (e.g., gymnastics, figure skating) are the exception favoring earlier specialization.
  - DOK 2: The canonical early-diversify-then-narrow trajectory: plural and reversible early, converging by ~13. Timing is domain-dependent.
  - Link: https://doi.org/10.1123/tsp.13.4.395
- **Frontiers in Sports & Active Living (2023), early- vs late-specialization review.**
  - DOK 1: "Predictors of rapid junior performance and of long-term senior performance are opposite"; most elite athletes specialize in mid-to-late adolescence.
  - DOK 2: Early single-domain lock-in is neither necessary nor safe for elite outcomes — an argument against irreversible early specialization, not against an early *start*.
  - Link: https://doi.org/10.3389/fspor.2023.1175718
- **Watanabe, Savion-Lemieux & Penhune (2007), "The effect of early musical training on adult motor performance," Experimental Brain Research 176(2), 332–340; Steele, Bailey, Zatorre & Penhune (2013), J. Neuroscience 33(3), 1282–1290.**
  - DOK 1: Musicians who began before age 7 outperform later-starters on sensorimotor timing/synchronization *even when matched for total years of training*; early starters show enhanced corpus-callosum white matter, with earlier onset → greater connectivity.
  - DOK 2: A genuine sensitive period (~before 7) for motor/rhythm skill. For such candidates, minimum early dosage matters — a start-timing exception, not a lock-in exception.
  - Links: https://doi.org/10.1007/s00221-006-0619-z ; https://doi.org/10.1523/JNEUROSCI.3578-12.2013

### Category F — Grit, mindset & transfer (the switch-vs-quit substrate)

- **Duckworth, Peterson, Matthews & Kelly (2007), "Grit: Perseverance and passion for long-term goals," JPSP 92(6), 1087–1101.**
  - DOK 1: Grit = perseverance of effort + consistency of interest; ~4% of variance in success outcomes; correlated with conscientiousness, not IQ. Consistency items score interest change ("My interests change from year to year") as a deficit; the gritty person "does not swerve … even in the absence of positive feedback."
  - DOK 2: The original construct explicitly penalizes switching. Useful, but small-effect and conflates two separable facets.
  - Link: https://doi.org/10.1037/0022-3514.92.6.1087
- **Credé, Tynan & Harms (2017), "Much ado about grit: A meta-analytic synthesis," JPSP 113(3), 492–511.**
  - DOK 1: 584 effect sizes, 88 samples, 66,807 people. Higher-order grit structure not confirmed; grit ≈ conscientiousness (ρ ≈ .84); *perseverance of effort* has significantly stronger criterion validity than *consistency of interest*; grit interventions likely weak.
  - DOK 2: Only the effort facet earns its keep. Demanding domain loyalty ("consistency") from a child is unsupported; demanding effort within the current attempt is supported.
  - Link: https://doi.org/10.1037/pspp0000102
- **O'Keefe, Dweck & Walton (2018), "Implicit theories of interest: Finding your passion or developing it?" Psychological Science 29(10), 1653–1664.**
  - DOK 1: A *fixed* theory of interest makes people lose interest when the work gets hard; a *growth* theory sustains engagement through difficulty.
  - DOK 2: The reason a child gives for quitting is diagnostic: a fixed/identity verdict at a difficulty spike is the avoidance signal to catch.
  - Link: https://doi.org/10.1177/0956797618780643
- **Sala & Gobet (2017), "Does far transfer exist? Negative evidence…," Current Directions in Psychological Science 26(6), 515–520.**
  - DOK 1: Far-transfer effects collapse to near-zero under proper controls (illustrative second-order pattern: g ≈ .88 trained, .27 near, .12 far).
  - DOK 2: Skill doesn't compound across domains on its own — so each concurrent spike is a full, separate time cost (basis for the plurality cap).
  - Link: https://doi.org/10.1177/0963721417712760

## Recommendation for GT100K

### A. Definitions (so "how many" is unambiguous)

- **Spike** = a candidate pursuit receiving *protected depth-hours* and tracked for *delayed voluntary return* (the parent doc's core signal).
- **Sampler** = low-dose exposure, not yet a spike.
- **Dilution** = the state where spikes compete for the same protected hours such that *none* clears the re-engagement threshold. The plurality cap exists to prevent this (far transfer won't rescue you — Sala & Gobet 2017).
- **Park** = a spike set to reversible dormant status: returnable anytime, never deleted, no penalty. This is the default "off-ramp," chosen over "quit."

### B. Plurality policy — spikes by age

| Age band | Concurrent **spikes** (protected depth) | Samplers | Posture | Why |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **6–8 · Discover** | up to **3**, all light-dose | wide open | Breadth + deliberate play; switching essentially free | No sunk cost yet (Sehl); tilt unmeasurable (Achter); sampling doesn't hurt (Côté) |
| **9–11 · Narrow** | **2** (1 lead + 1 second) | 1 rotating slot | Begin switch protocol; use emerging tilt to pick the lead | Sunk cost + identity coming online; tilt now more resolvable (Achter) |
| **12–14 · Specialize** | **1 lead + 1 protected second** | occasional | Concentrate depth **but** mandate re-choice; parking allowed | Foreclosure + sunk-cost risk peak; second spike is anti-foreclosure insurance (Marcia; Kroger; Coakley) |

Rules attached to the table:
1. **Hard ceiling of 3, ever.** Beyond ~3 concurrent pursuits, breadth flips to net-negative (Fredricks 2012; Busseri 2006) and dilution sets in.
2. **The "second spike" is mandatory at 12–14, not optional.** It is the structural guarantee against a unidimensional, foreclosed identity — the exact burnout pathway Coakley (1992) describes. Never allow a sole spike in this band without an active second pursuit or a live sampler rotation.
3. **Exploration is a scheduled requirement, not a mood.** Run a periodic **re-choice checkpoint** (suggest ~every 6 months) where continuing the lead is an active, low-stakes *re-decision*, not a default. Exploration does not happen on its own (Kroger 2010).
4. **Sensitive-period exception.** For motor/rhythm/music candidates (and early-peak sports), protect a *minimum early dosage* before ~age 7 even while other spikes churn (Watanabe 2007; Steele 2013; Côté). This governs start dosage only — it never overrides reversibility.

### C. Reversibility policy — when to allow vs gently resist

- **Default = allow, via Park.** Any switch/park request is honored after the protocol below. Prior investment of time, money, or identity is **never** a valid reason to keep a child in a spike — that is the adult's sunk-cost bias, empirically absent in the child (Sehl; Arkes & Ayton).
- **Gently resist in exactly two situations, and only by scaffolding — never by forbidding:**
  1. **Hot state** — the request comes in the trough of a single frustrating session. → Impose a **cool-down** (decide after ~2 weeks / a few sessions), don't decide in the trough.
  2. **First wall** — the child is bailing at the *first* real difficulty, having cleared none. → **Scaffold the obstacle** (competence support) and **reframe** with growth-of-interest language, then re-offer the choice.
- Everywhere else: allow/park immediately. Resistance is a cooling-and-scaffolding gate, not a veto.

### D. The decision rule — healthy switch vs quitting-when-it-gets-hard

> **A switch is HEALTHY when it is a cool, approach-driven move *toward* a domain the child already voluntarily returns to, made *after* clearing at least one real difficulty in the current spike. It is AVOIDANCE when it is a hot, away-from-difficulty exit at the first wall, justified by an identity verdict ("I'm not a ___ person").**

Operationalize as a 4-gate check (each gate ties to evidence):

1. **Cool-down gate (state).** Not decided in an emotional trough. *(sunk-cost/affect: Sehl 2024; Arkes & Ayton 1999)*
2. **Effort gate (perseverance, not loyalty).** Has the child pushed through ≥1 genuine obstacle in this spike? If they bail at the first wall → treat as avoidance: scaffold first. We tax *effort within the attempt*, never *domain constancy*. *(Credé 2017; Duckworth 2007)*
3. **Pull gate (approach vs escape).** Is there pre-existing *voluntary return* to the destination (a real pull toward), or is the move purely *away from* pain? Approach = green; pure escape = yellow → cool-down + scaffold. *(delayed-return signal; O'Keefe 2018)*
4. **Language gate (reason given).** Fixed/identity verdict ("I'm bad at this / not a math person") = coach the mindset before honoring. Approach statement ("I keep choosing Y / I want to go deeper on Y") = honor. *(O'Keefe, Dweck & Walton 2018)*

**Resolution:** Pass gates 2–4 (after gate 1) → **Park the old spike, switch honored.** Fail on effort/language → **not a veto**: scaffold the obstacle, reframe, cool down, then re-offer; if the child still chooses to leave after clearing one real difficulty in a cool state, **honor it** — reversibility that isn't real isn't reversibility (Vallerand 2003; Mageau 2009). Default the exited spike to **Parked**, never deleted, keeping the profile plural and non-foreclosing.

### E. Guardrails / honest limits to hold

- Do **not** convert this into a "no-quit" regime under the banner of grit; the effort facet is real, the loyalty facet isn't (Credé 2017).
- Reversibility needs **re-engagement scaffolding** or it becomes drift; pair every open exit with active competence support on the current spike.
- Much of the passion/grit/identity evidence is adolescent/adult; treat the 6–10 applications as **[extrapolated]** and re-check against the strong in-band anchors (child sunk-cost, sport sampling, music sensitive period).
- "Park, don't delete" is a design bet on removing the sunk-cost/foreclosure charge; monitor whether parked spikes are ever genuinely resumed, or whether "park" quietly becomes "quit with extra steps."
