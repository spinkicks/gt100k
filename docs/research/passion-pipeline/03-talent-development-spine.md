# Passion Pipeline — 03: The Talent-Development Spine

**Subtitle:** Turning a validated spike into world-class expertise in a child, ages 6–14.
**Scope:** How specialization should be *staged* once discovery hands over a validated spike (candidate domain + work-mode profile). Discovery, spike-validation, motivation measurement, and the Evidence Graph provenance machinery are covered in the sibling memos and the master `passionBrainlift.md`; this memo owns the *ascent* — the pathway from "genuine interest" to "world-class trajectory."

---

## 1. Thesis

A validated spike becomes world-class not through an early grind but through a **staged, mentor-relayed climb** — *playful ignition → bounded precision → authentic authorship → signature integration* — where the program's honest job by age 14 is not to manufacture an expert but to deliver a **"ready-to-invest" performer** who owns the psychosocial skills (not just the hours) to go the rest of the distance.

---

## 2. DOK 4 — Spiky Points of View

Each breaks with a live consensus and survives contact with the evidence. Every one is bounded by an honest limit.

### SPOV 1 — By 14 the deliverable is a *ready-to-invest performer*, not an expert. The heavy "investment years" belong to 16+.

- **Elaboration:** The hothouse instinct is "start at 6, grind hard, arrive early." The developmental-sport evidence says the opposite about *timing*: the heavy, single-domain, deliberate-practice "investment years" are an adolescent phenomenon (~16+), and the childhood "sampling years" (6–12) are built on multi-activity play, not volume. Professional ice-hockey players in Soberlak & Côté logged only ~10% of their sport-specific hours during the sampling years and ~56% during the investment years — the grind came *late*, after the love and the base were secured. A program that ends at 14 is therefore, by construction, building **competency and a launch platform**, not eminence (which Subotnik et al. define as an adult outcome). If we forget this and front-load investment-year loads onto a 12-year-old, we don't buy time — we buy overuse injury, burnout, and the ~70% adolescent dropout the youth-sport literature documents.
- **Consensus it breaks:** "Elite outcomes require the earliest possible heavy specialization; more childhood hours = earlier expertise."
- **Backing:** Côté DMSP sampling/specializing/investment stages (Côté, 1999; Côté, Lidor & Hackfort, 2009); Soberlak & Côté hour-distribution (10% sampling vs 56% investment); AAP clinical report on early specialization → overuse/burnout and ~70% dropout by 13 (Brenner/AAP, 2016); Subotnik, Olszewski-Kubilius & Worrell (2011) — eminence is an adult, later-stage outcome.
- **Honest limit:** A minority of domains genuinely peak early (women's gymnastics, figure skating, some instrumental music and competition math), where compressed timelines are real. We treat these as flagged exceptions that *add* burnout guardrails, not as the default.

### SPOV 2 — Deliberate practice is a scalpel, not the engine. Dose it small, dose it late, and only ever inside a child-owned project.

- **Elaboration:** The "10,000-hour" story treats deliberate practice (DP) as the master variable. The best meta-analysis says it is *a* variable: DP explained 26% of performance variance in games, 21% in music, 18% in sports, and only 4% in education — leaving most of the variance to ability, opportunity, mentoring, timing, and psychosocial skill. Worse for our age band: Ericsson himself specified that DP is "not inherently enjoyable" and that when the performer is a child, "someone in the [child's] environment" must supply the motivation and structure. Pair that with the reward-undermining evidence (worse in children than adults) and the design conclusion is forced: DP for a 6–14-year-old must be *small, embedded in a project the child chose, and framed as the price of a goal they own* — never a free-standing drill regime, never gamified with contingent rewards.
- **Consensus it breaks:** "Expertise is mostly accumulated deliberate-practice hours; maximize them early."
- **Backing:** Macnamara, Hambrick & Oswald (2014) meta-analysis; Ericsson, Krampe & Tesch-Römer (1993) on DP's motivational/effort/resource constraints and the child-support requirement; Côté's "deliberate play" as the age-appropriate early substitute; Deci, Koestner & Ryan (1999) reward-undermining (see master brainlift).
- **Honest limit:** DP is still the single most *trainable* lever inside a domain, and "small and late" must not collapse into "none." Under-practice fails too; the craft floor is real. The blueprint sets a rising DP fraction by stage precisely to avoid this.

### SPOV 3 — The mentor is not one person but a relay of three, and the most valuable thing each hands over is the *next* mentor.

- **Elaboration:** The romantic model is "find a great coach and stick with them for the journey." Bloom's study of 120 world-class performers found something more specific and more useful: talent is developed by a *sequence* of teachers with different jobs — a **warm first teacher** who makes the child fall in love, then a **technically demanding teacher** who builds precision, then a **master teacher** who shapes personal style. Critically, the child rarely reaches the master teacher on their own; the *second-phase teachers brokered the auditions* through their professional networks. The scarcest, most consequential mentor act is not instruction — it is *access transfer*: opening the door to the next tier, and to the "insider knowledge" (the field's tacit rules, standards, and gatekeepers) that Subotnik's megamodel says matters more at each higher stage.
- **Consensus it breaks:** "One mentor, matched well, carries a talent from novice to expert."
- **Backing:** Bloom (1985) three-teacher progression and network-brokered access to master teachers; Subotnik, Olszewski-Kubilius & Worrell (2011) on insider/tacit knowledge and mentoring increasing in importance across stages (and talented women leaving STEM partly for lack of mentors).
- **Honest limit:** Relays create *handoff risk* — a botched transition (wrong-timed, cold, or status-shocking) is exactly where pursuits die. The handoff must be engineered as a designed event, not left to luck; this is a real operational cost, not a freebie.

### SPOV 4 — Grade the child's psychosocial skills as deliberately as the craft. They are the actual rate-limiter, and they are teachable.

- **Elaboration:** Talent is usually modeled as ability × practice, with "soft skills" as a byproduct. The talent-development evidence inverts this: Subotnik et al. state that transitions *between* stages are "largely a function of developed psychosocial skills," and that these skills are **malleable and must be deliberately cultivated**. In sport and music, a specific, teachable set — Psychological Characteristics of Developing Excellence (PCDEs: commitment, goal-setting, imagery, planning, quality practice, realistic self-evaluation, coping with pressure, focus/distraction control, self-awareness) — discriminates good developers from poor ones (67–75% correct classification) and should be trained *early and systematically*, not bolted on at the elite level. If the Evidence Graph grades the process, the psychosocial curriculum is part of what the process should *show*.
- **Consensus it breaks:** "Develop the skill; character and coping will follow. Psychosocial skills are personality, not curriculum."
- **Backing:** Subotnik, Olszewski-Kubilius & Worrell (2011) — psychosocial skills as stage-transition determinants, malleable, deliberately cultivated; MacNamara, Button & Collins (2010) PCDEs and the discriminant validity of the PCDEQ; Csikszentmihalyi, Rathunde & Whalen (1993) — talent persists when its use produces flow inside a high-support/high-challenge ("complex") environment.
- **Honest limit:** The PCDE evidence base is sport/music and skews adolescent-to-adult; extending it *downward* to ages 6–10 and *sideways* into academic/maker domains is our extrapolation to validate, not a settled result.

### SPOV 5 — Protect the "rage to master" above all. The child's own obsessive pull is the scarce fuel, and adult pressure is what extinguishes it.

- **Elaboration:** Winner's study of gifted children found that the ones who go far carry a **"rage to master"** — an intrinsic, near-obsessive drive to make sense of a domain, coupled with frequent flow states. That drive is the engine that makes the (unenjoyable) deliberate practice survivable. It is also fragile: contingent extrinsic rewards, adult control, and competition-for-its-own-sake reliably corrode intrinsic motivation, and the damage is *worse in children*. So the specialization design's first duty is negative — do not kill the fuel. Meter pressure, keep the pursuit reversible and autonomy-supported, and treat time-losing absorption as the signal to feed, not a symptom to manage.
- **Consensus it breaks:** "Motivation is an output you manufacture with rewards, rankings, and parental push."
- **Backing:** Winner (1996) rage to master + flow; Winner & Drake (2018) on intrinsic motivation as necessary for the practice mastery requires; Deci, Koestner & Ryan (1999) reward-undermining; Vallerand et al. (2003) / Mageau et al. (2009) harmonious vs obsessive passion (see master brainlift); Csikszentmihalyi et al. (1993) flow.
- **Honest limit:** Our target user is a child with *low agency to explore alone* — so pure non-intervention under-develops them. The needle to thread is adding **structure and opportunity without adding control**; that is the hardest design constraint in the whole spine, and the one most likely to fail in practice.

### SPOV 6 — Progression is measured by *widening the audience*, not by adding hours. The unit of the spine is the authentic project.

- **Elaboration:** The default progression variable is volume (more hours, harder drills). We make the progression variable **audience and authenticity**: a child levels up when their work moves from *made-for-myself* → *made-for-my-mentor* → *made-for-a-real-community* → *made-for-the-field*. This is Renzulli's Type III — a self-selected investigation of a real problem, using authentic methodology, with no pre-existing right answer, *designed to affect a real audience* — as the recurring unit of the spine, with the teacher acting as mentor/guide rather than instructor. Ambition scales by who the work is for, which is also what makes the Evidence Graph legible to an outside evaluator and what elite admissions actually reward.
- **Consensus it breaks:** "You build skill through curriculum and drills first, then 'apply' it later in projects."
- **Backing:** Renzulli (1977) Enrichment Triad, Type III's four authenticity criteria and the teacher-as-mentor shift; Bloom (1985) on identity forming through *doing* (children saw themselves as "pianists"/"swimmers" before 11–12); ties to the Evidence Graph (grade the process) in the master brainlift.
- **Honest limit:** Type III presumes prerequisite skills (Renzulli's Type I/II). Audience-widening with no craft floor produces confident shallowness; each project must be paired with the bounded DP of SPOV 2, or ambition outruns competence.

## 3. DOK 3 — Insights (evidence → blueprint)

Original conclusions that bridge the Knowledge Tree to the blueprint.

- **Insight 1 — The three canonical staged models are the *same spine* under three vocabularies.** Subotnik's *potential → competency → expertise → eminence*, Bloom's *early → middle → later years*, and Côté's *sampling → specializing → investment years* line up almost cleanly. We adopt the union as a single four-stage architecture rather than picking one. (→ Blueprint; SPOV 1)
- **Insight 2 — Stages are gated by *experience*, not age.** Sosniak (in Bloom) argued the amount of prior experience with a domain is at least as good a guide to instruction as the learner's age, and that the phases are pedagogically, not biologically, ordered. So the blueprint uses indicative age bands but *advances children on evidence of readiness*, not birthdays. (→ Blueprint stage-gates)
- **Insight 3 — The single most actionable talent finding is Bloom's teacher relay.** Warm-then-technical-then-master, with each teacher brokering the next. We productize this as an explicit, designed *mentor handoff* with access-transfer as a first-class deliverable. (→ SPOV 3; Blueprint "mentor role")
- **Insight 4 — Deliberate practice must rise as a *fraction* across stages, from near-zero to bounded.** Early stages are dominated by Côté's "deliberate play" (structured for enjoyment, flexible rules); the DP share increases with stage and readiness, but the program never reaches investment-year loads before 14. (→ SPOV 1/2; Blueprint "DP dose")
- **Insight 5 — Psychosocial skills are a graded curriculum, sequenced by stage.** PCDEs are deployed differently at different stages; we map specific PCDEs to each stage (relatedness/enjoyment early; goal-setting/quality-practice/self-evaluation mid; coping-with-audience/strategic-risk/self-advocacy late). (→ SPOV 4; Blueprint "psychosocial focus")
- **Insight 6 — The "learner → performer/producer" shift is a distinct, teachable transition, not a graduation side-effect.** Subotnik names it; Bloom shows identity ("I am a pianist") forming years before mastery. The blueprint makes it an explicit late-stage objective with its own supports. (→ SPOV 4/6; Stage 3–4)
- **Insight 7 — Flow is the design target that reconciles "bounded DP" with "protect the rage to master."** Set challenge ≈ skill (Vygotsky's ZPD operationalized), and practice produces flow instead of anxiety; Csikszentmihalyi shows talent persists precisely when its use is autotelic. (→ SPOV 2/5)
- **Insight 8 — The autonomy paradox is the program's core tension, not a footnote.** Our user has low agency; the evidence says control kills durable passion. The resolution is *scaffolded autonomy*: the adult supplies opportunity, structure, and access (which the child can't self-source) while the child keeps choosing the problem, the method, and the pace. (→ SPOV 5; Blueprint guardrails)
- **Insight 9 — Plural spikes run *parallel stage clocks*, not a shared one.** Because far transfer largely fails (Sala & Gobet), a second spike is a second real pursuit at its own stage, with its own mentor relay and cadence — never a discount funded by the first. (→ Blueprint "plurality")
- **Insight 10 — "Eminence by 14" is a category error we must design against.** Eminence is adult; the program's terminal artifact is a *signature body of work + Evidence Graph + defensible portfolio* that positions the child to enter the investment years well. Honest scope prevents the burnout that overreach guarantees. (→ SPOV 1; Stage 4)
- **Insight 11 — Ability still matters and should keep steering the domain.** The megamodel and SMPY (master brainlift) insist ability is necessary-not-sufficient and keeps mattering at the extreme; the spine assumes the validated spike already encodes an ability tilt, and re-checks fit at each stage-gate rather than treating the domain as locked. (→ Blueprint stage-gates)

## 4. DOK 2 — Knowledge Tree

DOK-1 facts are raw from the source; DOK-2 summaries are in our own words. All citations are real; DOIs/links included.

### Category 1 — Staged talent-development models

- **Source: Subotnik, Olszewski-Kubilius & Worrell (2011), "Rethinking Giftedness and Gifted Education," *Psychological Science in the Public Interest* 12(1), 3–54 — the Talent-Development Megamodel.**
   - **DOK 1 — Facts:**
      - Giftedness is developmental: "in the beginning stages, potential is the key variable; in later stages, achievement is the measure … and in fully developed talents, eminence is the basis." Four-stage arc: potential → competency → expertise → eminence.
      - Domains "have developmental trajectories that vary … with regard to when they tend to start, peak, and end."
      - "Psychosocial variables play an essential role in the manifestation of giftedness at every developmental stage," and are "malleable and need to be deliberately cultivated."
      - "The transitions across stages — especially transitions through the later stages into adulthood — are largely a function of developed psychosocial skills."
      - Opportunities must be *provided* by community/school *and taken up* by the individual; eminence is the aspired (adult) outcome.
   - **DOK 2 — Summary:**
      - This is the backbone: a validated interest becomes expertise via domain-specific, stage-sequential opportunity plus a deliberately built psychosocial curriculum, with mentoring/insider-knowledge weighting up at higher stages.
      - The endpoint (eminence) is adult, so a 6–14 program builds the *trajectory*, not the summit.
   - **Links:** https://doi.org/10.1177/1529100611418056 ; 2021 restatement: https://doi.org/10.1007/978-3-030-56869-6_24 ; related TAD framework (Preckel et al., 2020): https://doi.org/10.1177/1745691619895030

- **Source: Bloom, B. S. (Ed.) (1985), *Developing Talent in Young People*, Ballantine (ISBN 9780345315090); Sosniak, "Phases of Learning."**
   - **DOK 1 — Facts:**
      - Retrospective study of 120 world-class performers across six fields (concert pianists, sculptors, Olympic swimmers, tennis players, research mathematicians, research neurologists).
      - Three phases: **early years** — playful, "tinkering," many immediate rewards, a warm/encouraging first teacher; **middle years** — precision, discipline, systematic work under perfectionist technical teachers demanding much practice; **later years** — technical precision becomes personal expression under a "master teacher."
      - Master teachers accepted few students; access was *brokered by the second-phase teachers through their professional networks*.
      - Children "began to see themselves as 'pianists' and 'swimmers' before the age of 11 or 12" — identity preceded mastery.
      - "In the first period of formal instruction … motivation and effort count far more than do the particular gifts or special qualities of the child" ("drive, not talent").
      - Sosniak: phase sequence is pedagogically (not biologically) ordered; prior experience is at least as good an instructional guide as age.
   - **DOK 2 — Summary:**
      - The most concrete mentor design in the literature: a *relay* of three teacher-types, each with a distinct job, and access-transfer as the pivotal act.
      - Fall-in-love first, systematize second, individuate third — and gate on experience, not age.
   - **Link:** https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/15009/developing-talent-in-young-people-by-benjamin-bloom/

### Category 2 — The authentic-project spine

- **Source: Renzulli, J. S. (1977), "The Enrichment Triad Model," *Gifted Child Quarterly* 21(2), 227–233 (ERIC EJ169896); UConn Schoolwide Enrichment Model.**
   - **DOK 1 — Facts:**
      - Three activity types: Type I (general exploratory experiences), Type II (group training in thinking/creativity/methodology skills), **Type III** (individual/small-group investigations of real problems).
      - A "bona fide" Type III meets four criteria: (1) personalization of interest (the student chooses, with emotional commitment); (2) use of authentic methodology; (3) no pre-existing/"right" answer; (4) designed to have an impact on a real audience beyond the teacher.
      - In Type III the teacher's role shifts from instructor to "coach, mentor, guide-on-the-side"/resource-procurer; the student's role shifts "from learner to doer"; projects run over a long time and yield a real product.
      - Stated goals include developing planning, organization, resource use, time management, decision-making, self-evaluation, task commitment, self-confidence, and the ability to interact with advanced practitioners.
   - **DOK 2 — Summary:**
      - Type III is almost exactly our recurring project unit and mentor stance; audience authenticity (criterion 4) is the progression lever, and Type I/II are the prerequisite-skill scaffolds that keep projects from being shallow.
   - **Link:** https://gifted.uconn.edu/schoolwide-enrichment-model/the-enrichment-triad-model/ ; PDF: https://gifted.media.uconn.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/961/2022/06/The_Enrichment_Triad_Model.pdf

### Category 3 — Deliberate practice, properly bounded for children

- **Source: Ericsson, K. A., Krampe, R. T. & Tesch-Römer, C. (1993), "The role of deliberate practice in the acquisition of expert performance," *Psychological Review* 100(3), 363–406.**
   - **DOK 1 — Facts:**
      - Deliberate practice = effortful, structured activity *designed to improve performance*, with well-defined tasks, feedback, and repetition/error-correction.
      - It "is not inherently motivating"; performers do it for the *result*, not enjoyment (the "motivational constraint").
      - It requires time, energy, teachers, materials, facilities (the "resource constraint") and effortful concentration (the "effort constraint").
      - "If the individual is a child or adolescent, someone in the [child's] environment must" supply motivation/structure.
      - Expert performance follows "a minimum of 10 years" of such practice (the popular "10,000-hour rule" is a later gloss, not Ericsson's claim).
   - **DOK 2 — Summary:**
      - DP works, but it is intrinsically un-fun and, for children, externally scaffolded by definition — which is exactly why it must be small, chosen, and project-embedded to avoid corroding intrinsic motivation.
   - **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-295X.100.3.363

- **Source: Macnamara, B. N., Hambrick, D. Z. & Oswald, F. L. (2014), "Deliberate Practice and Performance …: A Meta-Analysis," *Psychological Science* 25(8), 1608–1618.**
   - **DOK 1 — Facts:**
      - Variance in performance explained by DP: **26% games, 21% music, 18% sports, 4% education, <1% professions**; overall ~12% (2018 corrigendum: 24/23/20/5/1%).
      - Domain and task-predictability were significant moderators (more predictable domains → more explained by DP).
      - Conclusion: "deliberate practice is important, but not as important as has been argued."
   - **DOK 2 — Summary:**
      - Hours are one lever among several; most of the variance sits with ability, opportunity, mentoring, timing, and psychosocial skill. Don't build the spine around hour-maximization.
   - **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1177/0956797614535810 (corrigendum: https://doi.org/10.1177/0956797618769891)

- **Source: Côté, J. (1999), "The Influence of the Family in the Development of Talent in Sport," *The Sport Psychologist* 13(4), 395–417; Côté, Lidor & Hackfort (2009), ISSP position stand, *Int. J. Sport & Exercise Psychology* 7(1), 7–17.**
   - **DOK 1 — Facts:**
      - Developmental Model of Sport Participation (DMSP): **sampling years (~6–12)** — many activities + "deliberate play"; **specializing years (~13–15)** — fewer activities, balance of play and practice; **investment years (~16+)** — one domain, high deliberate practice.
      - "Deliberate play" (Côté & Hay, 2002) = activities *less structured, designed to maximize inherent enjoyment, regulated by flexible age-adapted rules* — the developmental counterpart to DP.
      - Coaching shifts: sampling = supportive/encouraging; specializing = more skill-oriented/technical with reciprocal coach–athlete respect.
      - Soberlak & Côté (2003): pro ice-hockey players accumulated 3,072 sport-specific hours from age 6–20; ~459 h (~10%) during sampling years vs ~2,215 h (~56%) during investment years.
      - Postulate: only in late adolescence (~16) do most performers have the maturity to *choose* and sustain investment-level specialization.
   - **DOK 2 — Summary:**
      - Play-vs-practice is a *dial that turns with stage*, not a binary; the heavy grind is developmentally late, and early enjoyment is the load-bearing wall for later intrinsic motivation.
   - **Links:** https://doi.org/10.1123/tsp.13.4.395 ; https://doi.org/10.1080/1612197X.2009.9671889

- **Source: Brenner, J. S. & AAP Council on Sports Medicine and Fitness (2016), "Sports Specialization and Intensive Training in Young Athletes," *Pediatrics* 138(3), e20162148.**
   - **DOK 1 — Facts:**
      - Early single-sport specialization is associated with increased overuse injuries, overtraining, burnout, anxiety, depression, and attrition.
      - Guidance: participate in multiple sports at least until puberty; take ≥1–2 days off per week; take ~3 months per year off the primary activity (in ~1-month increments) for physical/psychological recovery.
      - Context stats cited: overuse = ~46–50% of injuries; ~70% of children drop out of organized sport by age 13; ~3–11% of high-schoolers reach college level; ~0.03–0.5% reach professional level.
   - **DOK 2 — Summary:**
      - A hard health/attrition guardrail: early lock-in and volume without rest cause injury and dropout. Mandate diversification, rest cycles, and enjoyment; the health evidence and the motivation evidence point the same way.
   - **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1542/peds.2016-2148

### Category 4 — Motivation and the "rage to master"

- **Source: Winner, E. (1996), *Gifted Children: Myths and Realities*, Basic Books; Winner & Drake (2018), *Journal of Expertise* 1(1).**
   - **DOK 1 — Facts:**
      - Gifted children who progress show a **"rage to master"**: intrinsic, obsessive interest, intense focus, and frequent flow states while learning the domain.
      - Winner argues *both* are required: innate potential *and* an "enriched environment" of supportive caregivers who value the child's efforts; neither alone suffices.
      - Winner & Drake (2018): DP itself "requires intense intrinsic motivation" — the rage to master is what makes the (un-fun) practice sustainable.
   - **DOK 2 — Summary:**
      - The child's own obsessive pull is the scarce, non-manufacturable fuel; the program's job is to protect it and surround it with support/opportunity, not to substitute pressure for it.
   - **Link:** https://www.journalofexpertise.org/articles/JoE_2018_1_1_Winner_Drake_Apr10.pdf

- **Source: Csikszentmihalyi, M., Rathunde, K. & Whalen, S. (1993), *Talented Teenagers: The Roots of Success and Failure*, Cambridge University Press (ISBN 0521415780).**
   - **DOK 1 — Facts:**
      - Longitudinal study of talented adolescents: those who stayed committed were those for whom *using* the talent produced **flow** (challenge ≈ skill).
      - "Complex" family environments — simultaneously *supportive* and *stimulating/challenging* — bred "autotelic" personalities who seek challenge and build skill.
      - Teachers who sustained talent created "autotelic," self-rewarding classrooms, conveyed genuine enthusiasm, and *minimized* pressure from competition, grades, and needless rules.
   - **DOK 2 — Summary:**
      - Design the mentor/family environment to be "complex" (high support + high challenge together), and engineer tasks to produce flow; that is what converts ability into sustained development.
   - **Link:** https://archive.org/details/talentedteenager0000csik_v3s4

### Category 5 — Mentorship and the psychosocial-skill curriculum

- **Source: MacNamara, Á., Button, A. & Collins, D. (2010), "The Role of Psychological Characteristics in Facilitating the Pathway to Elite Performance," Parts 1 & 2, *The Sport Psychologist* 24(1), 52–73 and 74–96; PCDEQ validation.**
   - **DOK 1 — Facts:**
      - Psychological Characteristics of Developing Excellence (**PCDEs**) include: commitment, goal-setting, imagery, planning/organization, quality practice, realistic performance evaluation, coping with pressure, focus/distraction control, self-awareness — trait characteristics *and* state-deployed skills.
      - PCDEs matter throughout development, but *how they are deployed depends on stage, domain, and the individual* (Part 2).
      - The authors argue TID programs should build PCDEs *early and systematically*; the PCDEQ correctly classifies ~67–75% of athletes as good vs poor developers.
      - Parallel findings in music (MacNamara, Holmes & Collins, 2006, 2008).
   - **DOK 2 — Summary:**
      - Psychosocial skill is a concrete, assessable, stage-sequenced curriculum — not personality. We adopt PCDEs as the named skill set the Evidence Graph should surface, mapped stage-by-stage.
   - **Links:** https://doi.org/10.1123/tsp.24.1.52 ; https://doi.org/10.1123/tsp.24.1.74 ; music: https://doi.org/10.1177/0305735607086041

- **Source: Vygotsky, L. S. (1978), *Mind in Society*, Harvard University Press — Zone of Proximal Development.**
   - **DOK 1 — Facts:**
      - Learning is maximized in the "zone of proximal development": the gap between what a learner can do alone and what they can do with expert guidance/scaffolding.
   - **DOK 2 — Summary:**
      - The operational rule for the mentor and for DP dosing: pitch tasks just beyond independent reach, then fade support — the same challenge≈skill setting that produces flow.
   - **Link:** https://www.hup.harvard.edu/books/9780674576292 (Mind in Society)

### Category 6 — The transfer boundary (why plurality ≠ discount)

- **Source: Sala, G. & Gobet, F. (2017), "Does Far Transfer Exist? Negative Evidence …," *Current Directions in Psychological Science* 26(6), 515–520.**
   - **DOK 1 — Facts:**
      - Across chess, music, and working-memory training, far-transfer effects shrink to near-zero once active/placebo controls and publication bias are handled.
   - **DOK 2 — Summary:**
      - Depth in one spike does not automatically accelerate another; each plural spike is a separate pursuit on its own stage clock. (Fuller treatment in the master brainlift.)
   - **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1177/0963721417712760

## 6. Recommendation for GT100K — The Staged Specialization Blueprint

**Design stance:** one spine, four stages, gated by *experience/readiness* (not age). A validated spike enters at Stage 1. Each plural spike runs its **own** copy of this spine in parallel (no transfer discount). The program's terminal artifact at ~14 is a **ready-to-invest performer**: a signature body of work, a tamper-evident Evidence Graph of the process, and the psychosocial toolkit to enter the investment years (16+) that lie beyond us.

### 6.1 The four stages at a glance

| Stage | Indicative age band* | Megamodel / Bloom / Côté mapping | Mentor role (the relay) | Project cadence & audience | Deliberate-practice dose | Lead psychosocial skills (PCDEs) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **S1 — Ignition** ("fall in love") | ~6–9 / first 12–18 mo post-validation | potential · early years · sampling | **Warm first mentor** — encouraging, relatedness-first; protects the rage to master | Many short (2–4 wk) playful micro-projects; audience = *self* (+ family). High deliberate *play* | Near-zero formal DP; play-based skill exposure | Enjoyment, relatedness, identity spark ("I do X"), basic self-regulation |
| **S2 — Foundations** ("get precise") | ~9–11 | competency · middle years (early) · late sampling | **Technical mentor** — introduces standards, correction, feedback; sets challenge≈skill (ZPD) | Fewer, term-length projects (2–3/yr); audience = *mentor + peers*; Type II skill-building appears | Small, bounded DP embedded in projects; rising fraction; rest cycles enforced | Goal-setting, quality practice, planning, realistic self-evaluation, coping with first plateau; navigate the early-adolescent motivation dip |
| **S3 — Authorship** ("make it real") | ~11–13 | expertise (forming) · middle→later years · specializing | **Domain-expert mentor** — transmits *insider knowledge*, arranges real-audience opportunities, brokers next tier | 1–2 major Type III authentic investigations/yr; audience = *real community/field*; Evidence Graph captures process | Moderate, targeted DP in service of the project; still capped below investment loads | Strategic risk-taking, coping with public feedback/criticism, self-advocacy, "psychological armor," the learner→producer shift begins |
| **S4 — Signature** ("find your voice") | ~13–14+ (→ hand off to investment years 16+) | expertise→eminence *trajectory* · later years · specializing→pre-investment | **Master mentor** — treats child as near-peer apprentice; shapes personal style; opens field networks | One flagship, portfolio-defining project + coherent body of evidence + public five-minute defense | Highest in-program DP, still bounded; child self-directs the regime | Autonomy/ownership, self-directed goal-setting, resilience under stakes, managing competition, networking/insider access |

\* Age bands are indicative. **Advance on evidence of readiness** (Sosniak/Bloom): voluntary return, craft-floor mastery, and demonstrated psychosocial readiness for the next stage — not on birthday.

### 6.2 What happens in each stage

- **S1 — Ignition.** The only job is to make the child fall in love and keep coming back. Deliberate *play* dominates; formal practice is near-zero. The mentor is warm and encouraging (Bloom's first teacher). Success is measured by **voluntary return** (the Passion Engine's signal), never by output or reward. Guardrail: no contingent extrinsic rewards (reward-undermining is worst at this age).
- **S2 — Foundations.** Introduce precision without killing the fun. A technically skilled mentor sets standards and gives feedback-rich correction, pitching tasks in the ZPD so practice yields flow, not anxiety. DP appears but stays **small, chosen, and project-embedded**. This is where PCDEs become an explicit curriculum (goal-setting, quality practice, self-evaluation) and where the natural early-adolescent motivation dip is expected and buffered.
- **S3 — Authorship.** The spine's center of gravity: full Renzulli **Type III** projects — self-chosen real problems, authentic methods, no right answer, built for a **real audience**. The mentor is now a domain expert who transmits *insider knowledge* (how the field actually works, its standards and gatekeepers) and, crucially, *opens doors*. Ambition scales by **widening the audience**, not by adding hours. The Evidence Graph records the messy real process (grade the process, not the polish).
- **S4 — Signature.** The child develops a personal voice and a signature, portfolio-defining body of work under a **master mentor** who relates to them as a near-peer. This stage completes the **learner→producer shift**, produces the artifact + Evidence Graph + defensible portfolio that elite admissions reward, and readies the child for the true investment years that follow the program. Honest scope: this is an **expertise trajectory**, not eminence (which is adult).

### 6.3 The mentor relay (make handoffs first-class)

Bloom's finding, productized: the mentor is a **sequence** (warm → technical → expert → master), and the highest-value mentor act is **access-transfer** — brokering the next tier and its insider knowledge. Engineer each handoff as a *designed event* (overlap period, warm introduction, explicit "why now"), because botched transitions are where pursuits die. Track "access transferred" (introductions, auditions, real-audience placements) as a mentor deliverable, not a nicety.

### 6.4 Deliberate-practice dosing rules (bounded Ericsson)

1. **DP always serves a child-owned goal**, embedded in a project — never a free-standing drill regime.
2. **DP fraction rises with stage** (≈0 in S1 → highest-but-still-capped in S4); Côté's deliberate *play* is the early substitute.
3. **Never reach investment-year loads before 14.** Enforce AAP-style rest: ≥1–2 days/week off, ~3 months/year off the primary spike (in ~1-month increments).
4. **Set challenge ≈ skill** (ZPD) so DP produces flow, not burnout.
5. **No contingent extrinsic rewards** on intrinsically interesting work (protects the rage to master).
6. **Don't over-index on hours** — the meta-analysis says most variance is elsewhere; invest equally in mentoring, opportunity, and psychosocial skill.

### 6.5 Building psychosocial skills (the PCDE curriculum, staged)

Treat PCDEs as a graded, assessable curriculum the Evidence Graph surfaces: **S1** relatedness, enjoyment, identity, basic self-regulation → **S2** goal-setting, quality practice, planning, realistic self-evaluation → **S3** coping with public feedback, strategic risk-taking, self-advocacy → **S4** self-direction, resilience under stakes, networking, and the explicit learner→producer identity shift. Design the mentor+family environment to be **"complex"** (high support *and* high challenge together), per Csikszentmihalyi.

### 6.6 Guardrails and honest limits (design against these)

- **The autonomy paradox (highest risk).** Our user has low agency, but control kills durable passion. Resolve with **scaffolded autonomy**: adults supply opportunity/structure/access; the child keeps choosing problem, method, and pace.
- **Extrapolation flags.** The staged models are retrospective (Bloom) or from sport/music (Côté, PCDEs, AAP); downward extension to ages 6–10 and sideways into academic/maker domains is **ours to validate**, not settled.
- **Scope honesty.** Eminence is adult; promising it by 14 guarantees burnout. Ship a trajectory, not a summit.
- **Plurality is not a discount.** Far transfer fails; each spike is a separate spine with its own relay and cadence.
- **Craft floor.** Audience-widening without prerequisite skills (Renzulli Type I/II + bounded DP) produces confident shallowness; pair every project with its skill scaffold.
- **Early-peak exceptions.** A few domains peak early; handle with compressed timelines *plus* extra burnout guardrails, never as the default.
