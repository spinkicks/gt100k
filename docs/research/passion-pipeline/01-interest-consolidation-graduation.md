# Interest Consolidation → Specialization: The Graduation Gate

**Research memo · GT100K Passion Product · Brainlift format (DOK 4 → DOK 1)**
**Scope:** Evidence-based criteria for promoting a child (ages 6–14) from **Discovery** to **Specialization**.
**Author:** Research subagent · **Status:** v1 for design review

---

## 1. Thesis (one line)

**Graduate a child to specialization only when Discovery data shows the Hidi–Renninger *Phase 2 → Phase 3* crossing — operationalized as depth-weighted *self-initiated re-engagement that survives the removal of novelty and support* over a full term, corroborated by one "pushed-through-difficulty" artifact and a human check that the return is autonomous (harmonious), not pressured (obsessive).**

---

## 2. DOK 4 — Spiky POVs (synthesized hypotheses)

Each POV breaks with a common belief, is backed by real evidence, and carries an honest limit. Adult-sample evidence is flagged `[ADULT]`; ages-6–14 evidence is flagged `[6–14]`.

### POV 1 — The graduation event is a *named phase transition* (maintained situational → emerging individual interest), not a generic "engagement milestone."
Because we can name the boundary, we can define it with validated phase indicators (self-generated re-engagement, curiosity questions, perseverance, feedback-seeking, stored value) rather than arbitrary product metrics. Specialization (mentor-led PBL + deliberate practice) *requires* exactly the Phase-3 capacities; promoting a Phase-2 child hands them a grind they don't yet have the self-regulation to sustain.

- **Consensus it breaks:** That "the child has been very engaged for a while" is a sufficient promotion signal; that interest is a single scalar you threshold.
- **Backing:** Hidi & Renninger (2006) four-phase model — Phase 3 is defined as "a relatively enduring predisposition to *reengage* a particular class of content over time," with perseverance through frustration and self-set goals; Phases differ in affect, knowledge, *and value* [4-phase]. Renninger & Hidi (2016) publish explicit per-phase learner-behavior indicators usable for measurement.
- **Honest limit:** The phase model is descriptive and phase boundaries are fuzzy; it prescribes *which* behaviors to watch, not *how many* of them equal "crossed." The numbers are ours to set (see §6).

### POV 2 — The single highest-resolution signal is **motivation to re-engage after support is removed** — not time-on-task, streaks, or enjoyment ratings.
Build the gate around *unprompted return*, deliberately measured after you stop scaffolding and after the novelty "catch" has worn off.

- **Consensus it breaks:** Product-analytics instinct that total hours, streaks, or self-reported "I like it a lot" are the signal. Also breaks "if they're having fun, they're hooked."
- **Backing:** Boeder, Postlewaite, Renninger & Hidi (2021) — of the five Interest-Development-Scale factors (information-seeking, **motivation to re-engage**, persistence, self-regulation, value), *only* "motivation to re-engage" differentiated **all three** phases studied; the others separated only early vs. late `[ADULT, MTurk]`. Harackiewicz et al. (2008) — *enjoyment of lectures* (triggered-SI) did **not** predict later course choices, whereas *maintained* interest did, over seven semesters `[ADULT, college]`. Deci, Koestner & Ryan (1999) — the validated behavioral index of intrinsic motivation is *free-choice* (unprompted, unobserved) engagement `[mixed, incl. children]`.
- **Honest limit:** The IDS is an adult self-report scale; "re-engage after support removed" is our *behavioral* translation for children and has not been validated as a child threshold. Free-choice measures correlate only modestly with self-report.

### POV 3 — Durability is a **months-scale** property. 7-day / 30-day return windows are *novelty filters*, not graduation gates.
Use 7-day as "is this even alive," 30-day as a necessary screen (novelty has decayed), but gate promotion on a **term-length (~8–12 week) trajectory** spanning ≥2 Discovery review cycles.

- **Consensus it breaks:** The D7/D30 retention convention (and the PRD's own "7-day/30-day" framing) as if a month of returns proves a durable individual interest.
- **Backing:** Harackiewicz et al. (2008) measured the interest→choice link across **seven semesters** `[ADULT, college]`. Linnenbrink-Garcia et al. (2010) and Linnenbrink-Garcia, Patall & Messersmith (2013) show maintained-SI predicting change in *individual* interest **across a school year / 3-week-plus program**, in **middle- and high-school** samples `[6–14]`. The durable transitions in the literature are observed over terms, not days.
- **Honest limit:** Those studies sample interest via periodic self-report over terms, not daily behavioral logs; the *minimum* number of weeks for a 6-year-old is not pinned down empirically. Our 8–12 weeks is a theory-anchored default to calibrate.

### POV 4 — Require **BOTH voluntary return AND a perseverance-through-difficulty artifact.** Joy is a Phase-2 signal; effort-through-frustration is the Phase-3 signal that specialization actually consumes.
Do not promote on sustained enjoyment alone. Demand at least one Evidence-Graph artifact showing the child iterated *past* a failure or hard part.

- **Consensus it breaks:** "Follow the fun" / interest-first promotion; the assumption that positive affect predicts readiness to specialize.
- **Backing:** Hidi & Renninger (2006) / Renninger & Hidi (2016) — perseverance through frustration and pursuit of self-set goals mark *emerging individual* interest, not maintained situational. O'Keefe, Dweck & Walton (2018) — people who expect "boundless motivation" drop a new interest precisely when it gets hard; you want evidence the child *already* pushes through difficulty *before* you specialize `[ADULT]`. Alexander (2003) Model of Domain Learning — leaving "acclimation" requires sustained strategic effort, not just interest.
- **Honest limit:** O'Keefe's samples are adults/college. For 6–8s, "perseverance" is scaffold-dependent and looks different; applied rigidly, an artifact gate risks *under*-promoting young children (mitigated in §6 age rules).

### POV 5 — A human must gate promotion, and the human's primary job is **confound-busting** (is the return *autonomous?*), not cheerleading.
A frequency log cannot distinguish a child returning *because they love it* from one returning because of parental pressure, rewards, social defaults, or "nothing better on offer." That distinction is the whole ballgame.

- **Consensus it breaks:** The dream of a fully-automated, metrics-only promotion; and "more engagement is always better."
- **Backing:** Vallerand et al. (2003) — *controlled* internalization (pressure/contingencies) produces **obsessive** passion with rigid persistence and poor adaptation, indistinguishable from **harmonious** passion on a raw engagement count `[ADULT]`. Deci, Koestner & Ryan (1999) — external contingencies undermine intrinsic motivation, so someone must verify returns aren't reward/pressure-driven — *especially* in GT100K's high-commitment, co-engaged families `[mixed]`. Leibham et al. (2005) and Johnson et al. (2004) — parents are valid, informative observers of children's sustained interest `[6–14, early]`.
- **Honest limit:** Human judgment adds bias/variance; it must be a *structured rubric with multiple informants*. Harmonious/obsessive passion is an adult literature; downward extrapolation is reasonable but unvalidated for ages 6–14.

### POV 6 — Age **flips the dominant evidence source.** For 6–8, parent-observed at-home free-choice return is primary and child self-report should *not* gate; only by 12–14 can self-articulated value carry real weight.
One promotion rubric with age-scaled *weights*, not one uniform threshold.

- **Consensus it breaks:** Uniform criteria across 6–14; and "just ask the kid what they love."
- **Backing:** Boeder et al. (2021) validated self-report only in **adults**; young children's metacognition/self-report is immature. Alexander et al. (2008) & Johnson et al. (2004) — early interests are real but **fragile across the school transition**, and parent report + behavior are the workable signals `[6–14, ages 4–6]`. Linnenbrink-Garcia et al. (2010) validated three-component SI self-report from **middle school up (~11+)** `[6–14]`. DeLoache, Simcock & Macari (2007) — very early intense interests are strongly **gender-channeled**, so a breadth-of-exposure check must precede any "return = preference" claim `[under-6; flagged extrapolation]`.
- **Honest limit:** Parent report carries its own biases (projection, stereotype channeling); mitigate with direct behavioral logs and enforced exposure breadth.

## 3. DOK 3 — Insights (bridging evidence → our criteria)

1. **Name the boundary, then measure it.** "Graduation" = Phase 2 → Phase 3. This lets us translate a fuzzy "is it a real passion yet?" into a checklist of *validated phase indicators* (unprompted re-engagement, self-generated questions/goals, perseverance, feedback-seeking, stored value). (Hidi & Renninger 2006; Renninger & Hidi 2016.)

2. **"Voluntary return" must be engineered as *return-after-removal*.** The trustworthy signal isn't that the child keeps showing up while we keep serving novelty and scaffolding — it's that they come back *after we stop*. Operationalize with a deliberate **no-prompt gap** and a **novelty-decayed** baseline, scored on the specific **domain × work-mode** cell, depth-weighted. (Boeder 2021 "re-engage"; Deci 1999 free-choice; Harackiewicz 2008 maintained-not-triggered.)

3. **Run two clocks.** A *novelty-decay clock* (days → ~2 weeks) filters out "catch"; a *durability clock* (~8–12 weeks, ≥2 review cycles) certifies "hold." Promotion needs the durability clock, not just the novelty clock. (Harackiewicz 2008; Linnenbrink-Garcia 2010/2013.)

4. **Interest and competence are separable axes; gate on the conjunction.** Joy-without-skill → keep exploring (Phase 2). Skill-without-voluntary-return → do **not** force specialization (compliance/obsessive risk). Emerging individual interest is where both rise together. (Alexander 2003 MDL; Hidi & Renninger 2006; Vallerand 2003.)

5. **Autonomy is a hidden third axis invisible to logs.** Two identical return curves can be harmonious or obsessive. Only a human + multi-informant read can tell, so the human gate is *structural*, not optional. (Vallerand 2003; Deci 1999.)

6. **Keep the gate itself reward-neutral and reversible.** Promotion must not become a *prize* (that re-imports the overjustification effect and adds pressure → obsessive drift), and it must be framed as "current best bet, revisable," with an explicit off-ramp. Fixed "you found your passion" framing predicts collapse under difficulty. (Deci 1999; O'Keefe 2018; Alexander 2008 on interest fragility.)

7. **Scale evidence weights by age**, don't move one universal bar (see §6 table).

## 4. DOK 2 — Knowledge Tree (sources → raw facts + plain summary)

> Legend: **DOK-1** = raw fact from the source. **DOK-2** = plain-language summary/why it matters. Sample flags: `[6–14]`, `[ADULT]`, `[under-6]`.

### A. The phase model & its measurement (the backbone)

**Hidi, S., & Renninger, K. A. (2006). The four-phase model of interest development. *Educational Psychologist, 41*(2), 111–127.** https://doi.org/10.1207/s15326985ep4102_4 `[theory; 4,500+ citations]`
- **DOK-1:** Proposes four phases — *triggered situational → maintained situational → emerging individual → well-developed individual* — that are sequential, cumulative, differing in affect, knowledge, and value; later phases feature self-generated re-engagement, self-regulation, and perseverance.
- **DOK-2:** The canonical ladder. Our Discovery→Specialization gate sits at the maintained-situational → emerging-individual rung.

**Renninger, K. A., & Hidi, S. (2016). *The Power of Interest for Motivation and Engagement.* New York: Routledge. ISBN 9781138779792.** https://www.routledge.com/9781138779792 `[theory/measurement; note: some catalogs list ©2017]`
- **DOK-1:** Gives per-phase *learner-behavior indicators* (e.g., Phase 2: independently re-engages, has curiosity questions, self-regulates to own focus, stored knowledge/value; Phase 3: perseveres through frustration, reframes questions, actively seeks feedback).
- **DOK-2:** Turns the model into an observation checklist — directly reusable as our promotion rubric rows.

**Boeder, J. D., Postlewaite, E. L., Renninger, K. A., & Hidi, S. E. (2021). Construction and validation of the Interest Development Scale. *Motivation Science, 7*(1), 68–82.** https://doi.org/10.1037/mot0000204 `[ADULT, MTurk; n=348/515/103]`
- **DOK-1:** Five factors: information-seeking, **motivation to re-engage**, persistence, self-regulation, value. "Motivation to re-engage" **differentiated all three** phases studied; the other four separated only earlier vs. later phases.
- **DOK-2:** Empirical warrant for making *re-engagement* the primary, highest-resolution promotion signal — but it's an adult self-report; we must translate to child behavior.

### B. Maintained interest predicts later *choice* (the "it holds" evidence)

**Harackiewicz, J. M., Durik, A. M., Barron, K. E., Linnenbrink-Garcia, L., & Tauer, J. M. (2008). The role of achievement goals in the development of interest… *Journal of Educational Psychology, 100*(1), 105–122.** https://doi.org/10.1037/0022-0663.100.1.105 `[ADULT, college; N=858, 7 semesters]`
- **DOK-1:** *Situational* interest during an intro course — **independent of initial interest** — predicted subsequent course choices; interest in the course (≈ maintained-SI), **not** enjoyment of lectures (≈ triggered-SI), predicted number of courses taken and majoring.
- **DOK-2:** Promote on the *maintained* signal, not the novelty spark; the durable-choice horizon is terms/semesters.

**Linnenbrink-Garcia, L., Durik, A. M., Conley, A. M., Barron, K. E., Tauer, J. M., Karabenick, S. A., & Harackiewicz, J. M. (2010). Measuring situational interest in academic domains. *Educational and Psychological Measurement, 70*(4), 647–671.** https://doi.org/10.1177/0013164409355699 `[6–14; middle & high school, math]`
- **DOK-1:** Validated a three-factor SI structure — *triggered-SI*, *maintained-SI-feeling*, *maintained-SI-value* (12 items) — in middle/high schoolers; SI was distinct from individual interest and predicted change in individual interest across the school year.
- **DOK-2:** An age-appropriate (≈11+) self-report we can adapt as *corroboration* for 9–11 and 12–14, and a reminder that "feeling" and "value" are separate maintained sub-signals.

**Linnenbrink-Garcia, L., Patall, E. A., & Messersmith, E. E. (2013). Antecedents and consequences of situational interest. *British Journal of Educational Psychology, 83*(4), 591–614.** https://doi.org/10.1111/j.2044-8279.2012.02080.x `[6–14; 126 adolescents, mean age 14.6]`
- **DOK-1:** Perceived choice, instructor approachability, and real-life connections predicted SI; SI predicted end-of-program individual interest, perceived competence, and teacher-rated engagement, and *mediated* classroom-practice → outcome links.
- **DOK-2:** Confirms in-age that situational interest can convert to individual interest, and that *choice* and *mentor relationship* are levers — relevant to how Specialization should be introduced.

### C. Interests in early childhood (ages 6–8 grounding)

**Alexander, J. M., Johnson, K. E., Leibham, M. E., & Kelley, K. (2008). The development of conceptual interests in young children. *Cognitive Development, 23*(2), 324–334.** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cogdev.2007.11.004 `[6–14; 215 children, ages 4–6, 13 contacts/24 months]`
- **DOK-1:** First longitudinal analysis of prevalence/duration/intensity of conceptual interests ages 4–6; a substantial minority sustain them, but the probability of expressing a conceptual interest **declines significantly as formal schooling begins** (for both sexes); boys more likely than girls to hold conceptual interests.
- **DOK-2:** Young children's interests are real but **fragile and school-transition-sensitive** → for 6–8 use longer, repeated confirmation and don't over-read a dip as "interest gone."

**Johnson, K. E., Alexander, J. M., Spencer, S., Leibham, M. E., & Neitzel, C. (2004). Factors associated with the early emergence of intense interests within conceptual domains. *Cognitive Development, 19*(3), 325–343.** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cogdev.2004.03.001 `[6–14; 211 four-year-olds]`
- **DOK-1:** Child cognitive skills **plus** family factors (emphasis on consistency, communication, educational activities, and **time for free play**) predicted whether preschoolers *sustained* interests and built domain knowledge; boys ~6× more likely than girls to show such interests.
- **DOK-2:** Direct support for GT100K's family co-engagement and for protecting unstructured exploration time; family behavior is part of the maintenance mechanism, not noise.

**Leibham, M. E., Alexander, J. M., Johnson, K. E., Neitzel, C., & Reis-Henrie, F. P. (2005). Parenting behaviors associated with the maintenance of preschoolers' interests. *Journal of Applied Developmental Psychology, 26*(4), 397–414.** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.appdev.2005.05.001 `[6–14; prospective longitudinal]`
- **DOK-1:** Specific parenting behaviors prospectively predicted whether preschoolers' interests were maintained.
- **DOK-2:** Validates parents as informants and as an active support lever — central for the 6–8 evidence weighting.

**DeLoache, J. S., Simcock, G., & Macari, S. (2007). Planes, trains, automobiles—and tea sets: Extremely intense interests in very young children. *Developmental Psychology, 43*(6), 1579–1586.** https://doi.org/10.1037/0012-1649.43.6.1579 (correction: 2008, 44(1), 232, https://doi.org/10.1037/0012-1649.44.1.232) `[under-6; 177 parents — flagged extrapolation]`
- **DOK-1:** ~⅓ of young children show "extremely intense interests," emerging ~18 mo and often established ~24 mo; a **large gender difference** (more common in boys), with strongly stereotyped content.
- **DOK-2:** Intense early interest is common and can look impressive *without being a durable individual interest*; and it is heavily channeled → enforce **breadth of exposure** before treating "return" as a real preference. (Sample far below age 6; use as caution, not threshold.)

### D. Why Discovery is reward-neutral, and what "voluntary" means (mechanism guards)

**Deci, E. L., Koestner, R., & Ryan, R. M. (1999). A meta-analytic review of experiments examining the effects of extrinsic rewards on intrinsic motivation. *Psychological Bulletin, 125*(6), 627–668.** https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-2909.125.6.627 `[mixed; 128 experiments, incl. children]`
- **DOK-1:** Tangible, expected, contingent rewards **undermined free-choice intrinsic motivation** (d ≈ −0.28 to −0.40); positive *feedback* **enhanced** it (d ≈ +0.31–0.33); **tangible rewards were more detrimental for children than college students.** Intrinsic motivation is measured behaviorally as *free-choice* time on task.
- **DOK-2:** Triple duty: (1) justifies reward-neutral Discovery; (2) makes the *free-choice/voluntary-return* metric the right construct; (3) says use *informational feedback*, never tangible prizes — and be *extra* careful with the youngest.

**Vallerand, R. J., Blanchard, C., Mageau, G. A., Koestner, R., Ratelle, C., Léonard, M., Gagné, M., & Marsolais, J. (2003). Les passions de l'âme: On obsessive and harmonious passion. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 85*(4), 756–767.** https://doi.org/10.1037/0022-3514.85.4.756 `[ADULT; 4 studies, 900+ participants]`
- **DOK-1:** **Harmonious** passion (autonomous internalization) → adaptive outcomes, flexible engagement; **obsessive** passion (controlled internalization via internal/external pressure or contingencies) → rigid persistence, negative affect.
- **DOK-2:** The human gate must confirm returns are *autonomous*; an identical return curve can be healthy or harmful. Argues against pressure and against irreversible commitment.

**O'Keefe, P. A., Dweck, C. S., & Walton, G. M. (2018). Implicit theories of interest: Finding your passion or developing it? *Psychological Science, 29*(10), 1653–1664.** https://doi.org/10.1177/0956797618780643 `[ADULT/college; 5 studies]`
- **DOK-1:** A *fixed* theory of interest narrows exploration and causes people to drop a new interest when it becomes difficult; a *growth* theory sustains engagement through difficulty; fixed-theorists over-expect "boundless motivation."
- **DOK-2:** Backs the locked "revisable hypothesis, 1–3 spikes, never a fixed label" decision; and the requirement for a *perseverance* artifact + a reversible, "develop-it" framing at graduation.

**Alexander, P. A. (2003). The development of expertise: The journey from acclimation to proficiency. *Educational Researcher, 32*(8), 10–14.** https://doi.org/10.3102/0013189X032008010 `[theory; Model of Domain Learning]`
- **DOK-1:** Expertise develops across **acclimation → competence → proficiency**, driven by the interaction of *knowledge*, *strategic processing*, and *interest*; in acclimation, knowledge is fragmented and learners lean on **situational** interest; personal (individual) interest rises as competence consolidates.
- **DOK-2:** Gives the competence axis of our gate: promotion ≈ the child is leaving *acclimation* and personal interest is starting to carry the load — exactly when mentor-led deliberate practice becomes viable.

## 6. Recommendation for GT100K — concrete promotion criteria

### 6.1 Unit of analysis
Score Discovery signals per **(domain × work-mode) cell** (work-modes: build / investigate / compose / perform / debug / explain / persuade / collaborate / care). Promotion targets the **top-ranked cell** in the child's current spike hypothesis. Define a **Depth-Weighted Reengagement (DWR)** score per voluntary session ≈ *focused-minutes × challenge-level* (shallow clicks ≈ 0; effortful, self-extended work weighted up). Track **weekly DWR** per cell against the child's own rolling baseline.

### 6.2 The graduation gate (decision rule)
Promote **only if ALL behavioral gates hold AND a human signs off.** Stated interest may corroborate but **cannot substitute** for behavior + artifact.

**Behavioral gates (measured on the top cell):**
1. **Novelty-decay survival (necessary early filter):** unprompted return continues after a deliberately inserted **no-prompt / no-new-content gap of ≥10–14 days**. *(Rules out "catch.")*
2. **Durability (core gate):** DWR sustained across **≥8–12 weeks**, present in **≥⅔ of weeks** (e.g., ≥8 of trailing 12), spanning **≥2 Discovery review cycles**, with a **flat-or-rising** depth trend.
3. **Self-direction (≥2 of):** self-generated questions/goals; unprompted between-session information-seeking; self-initiated extension of a task beyond what was set.
4. **Perseverance artifact:** **≥1 Evidence-Graph artifact showing iteration *past* a failure/hard point** (not a one-shot polished output).
5. **Emerging competence:** a **measurable skill gain** in the cell (mentor-rated/rubric) — i.e., leaving "acclimation."
6. **Hypothesis stability + breadth:** the top cell is unchanged-or-strengthening across ≥2 cycles, **and** the child has demonstrably **sampled a diverse enough menu** that "return" reflects preference, not default.

**Human gate (structured, multi-informant — mentor + parent input):**
7. **Autonomy check:** return attributed to the activity itself, **not** rewards, adult pressure, social defaults, or absence of alternatives; **no obsessive-drive red flags** (distress when not doing it, rigid/compulsive persistence). Confirm harmonious, not obsessive.

> **All numeric thresholds above are theory-anchored engineering defaults, not empirically validated child cut-scores.** Anchor them to each child's own baseline and calibrate with your first cohorts. The *horizons* (weeks/terms) are the best-supported part (Harackiewicz 2008; Linnenbrink-Garcia 2010/2013); the *counts* are ours.

### 6.3 Keep the gate reward-neutral and reversible
- Frame promotion as **opportunity/identity** ("you're ready for a mentor and a real project"), **never a prize for hours** (Deci 1999). No points/streaks attached to being promoted.
- **Off-ramp (define before promoting):** if post-promotion DWR in the cell falls **below ~50% of its pre-promotion baseline for ≥3–4 consecutive weeks** — excluding a single mentor-judged "productive-struggle dip" — auto-flag for review and possible return to Discovery. Maintain the **"current best bet, revisable"** framing throughout (O'Keefe 2018; Vallerand 2003; Alexander 2008).

### 6.4 Age variations (scale the *weights*, not a single bar)

| Dimension | **6–8** | **9–11** | **12–14** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Primary evidence** | Parent-observed at-home **free-choice return** + mentor observation | Behavioral logs **+** light child self-report as corroboration | Behavioral logs **+** self-articulated **value & self-set goals** |
| **Child self-report** | **Not a gate** (metacognition immature; IDS is adult-validated) | Corroboration only (adapt Linnenbrink-Garcia SI items) | Meaningful corroboration (adapt IDS/SI items) |
| **Competence bar (gate 5)** | Low — curiosity/engagement, not skill | Real skill gain expected | Clear competence trajectory; nearest to deliberate practice |
| **Perseverance artifact (gate 4)** | Scaffolded; playful "tried again" counts | Semi-independent iteration | Independent iteration through difficulty |
| **Horizon** | Longer + repeated (interests fragile at school transition); re-confirm often | ~a term | ≥ a term; can commit hardest |
| **Spikes kept open** | 2–3, revisit frequently | 2–3, converging | May converge to 1, but keep off-ramp |
| **Specialization form** | Playful, low-pressure PBL | Balanced PBL + mentor | Full mentor-led PBL + deliberate practice |
| **Key risks to police** | Stereotype channeling (DeLoache); **no tangible rewards** (worst age for undermining, Deci 1999) | Over-reading a single strong cycle | **Premature foreclosure & obsessive passion** (O'Keefe; Vallerand) — autonomy/reversibility review most important |

### 6.5 What the human actually reviews (promotion dossier)
1. The **depth-weighted voluntary-return curve** (clears §6.2 gates 1–2? rising/stable? survived the no-prompt gap?).
2. The **Evidence-Graph artifacts** (self-initiated? iterated past difficulty? increasing complexity?).
3. A short **child conversation**: self-generated questions; wants to go deeper; attributes engagement to the activity (autonomous) vs. external reasons (controlled); harmonious vs. obsessive affect.
4. **Confound check**: rewards / adult pressure / social pull / "only option," plus **exposure-breadth** confirmation.
5. **Competence trajectory** + readiness for effortful, less-immediately-fun deliberate practice.
6. **Off-ramp acknowledgement**: promotion recorded as revisable, with the de-specialization trigger set.
