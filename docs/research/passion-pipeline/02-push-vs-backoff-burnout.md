# Passion Pipeline BrainLift 02: When to Push, When to Back Off, and How to Keep a Deepening Pursuit Healthy

## Purpose

This memo answers one operational question for the passion product: **when do we push a child harder, and when do we back off, as a built interest deepens toward world-class performance?** It converts the motivation, flow, passion, and burnout literatures into a concrete **signal → action** framework for both the human mentor and the system, plus a set of burnout guardrails. It assumes the pipeline's locked decisions and does not relitigate them: interest is **built not found**; the trustworthy signal is **voluntary, depth-weighted return**, never gamified; spikes stay **plural and reversible**; specialization runs as **PBL with a human mentor**; and we **measure behavior, not faces** (no facial/affect/emotion detection).

**Age note:** GT100K serves ages 6–14. The motivation and passion theories replicate in this band; the burnout early-warning evidence is strongest for **adolescent sport (roughly 12–18)** and thinner at 6–9. Adult- or older-adolescent-only findings are flagged inline as extrapolations.

---

## 1. Thesis (one line)

**Push the challenge, never the child: keep task difficulty in the ~80–90% success "stretch zone," and when strain appears, cut the *pressure and stakes* before you cut the *challenge* — because control (not difficulty) is what turns a harmonious passion into an obsessive one and ends in burnout, and the earliest behavioral warning is not exhaustion but quiet *devaluation* (the child still shows up but stops going deep).**

---

## 2. DOK 4 — Spiky Points of View

Each breaks with a live consensus and holds up on the evidence. Backing is real and cited; each carries an honest limit.

### SPOV 1 — "Back off" does not mean lower the bar. It means cut the pressure while keeping the challenge.

- **Elaboration:** Both camps in the parenting debate make the same mistake in opposite directions. The "push" camp raises challenge *and* pressure together; the "gentle" camp lowers both together. The evidence says these are two different levers with two different effects. High challenge, calibrated to the learner's edge, is what drives learning and flow (Wood, Bruner & Ross, 1976; Wilson et al., 2019). Interpersonal **control** — pressure, deadlines, surveillance, contingent approval — is what thwarts basic needs and predicts burnout, and it does so *independently* of how hard the task is (Bartholomew et al., 2011). So the correct move when a child strains under a serious pursuit is usually **demand-without-control**: hold the difficulty in the stretch zone and strip out the controlling delivery, rather than dumbing the work down. Grolnick & Pomerantz (2009) give the clean distinction: *structure* (clear expectations, guidance, rationale) is not *control* (pressure, intrusion, domination); structure helps competence, control harms it.
- **Consensus it breaks:** when it's too much for a kid, make it easier.
- **Backing:** Bartholomew et al. (2011), control → need thwarting → burnout, separable from challenge; Grolnick & Pomerantz (2009), structure ≠ control; Wilson et al. (2019) and Wood et al. (1976), challenge and scaffolding drive learning. **Honest limit:** in a live session, challenge and pressure are correlated and hard to cleanly separate, and even low-pressure high-challenge work needs periodic rest (see SPOV 5).

### SPOV 2 — The earliest behavioral warning of burnout is not exhaustion or tears. It is quiet devaluation: the child still shows up but stops going deep.

- **Elaboration:** People watch for the dramatic signs — meltdowns, "I'm exhausted," visible stress. But the athlete-burnout syndrome has three dimensions (emotional/physical exhaustion, reduced sense of accomplishment, and **sport devaluation** — a cynical, indifferent turn toward the activity), and the one most native to our own signal is devaluation, because it shows up **behaviorally** as declining voluntary return, compliance without depth, cancelled sessions, and no more self-initiated work (Raedeke & Smith, 2001). It is also the sign we can read without ever looking at a face. A child can be *exhausted and still passionate*; a child who has begun to **devalue** is the one drifting toward dropout — higher-burnout profiles predicted being 2.2–2.4× more likely to have quit six years later (Isoard-Gautheur, Guillet-Descas & Gustafsson, 2016). And obsessive passion hides exhaustion behind intensity (the kid who plays hurt), so intensity alone is not reassurance.
- **Consensus it breaks:** burnout announces itself as stress and fatigue; a busy, compliant, still-attending child is fine.
- **Backing:** Raedeke & Smith (2001), the devaluation dimension; Isoard-Gautheur et al. (2016), burnout profile → dropout six years out; Coakley (1992), burnout as constrained identity/control, not visible collapse. **Honest limit:** this evidence is adolescent sport; the 6–9 band is under-studied, and the three dimensions co-move, so read the *pattern* over time, not any single marker on a single day.

### SPOV 3 — Rising stakes should trigger *more* autonomy support, not less — the exact opposite of every adult's instinct.

- **Elaboration:** When a pursuit gets serious — a competition, a real audience, a specialization decision — the reflex of teachers, coaches, and parents is to tighten up: more control, more "now it counts," more pressure. That reflex is well-documented and it is backwards. The developmental fork between a **harmonious** passion (flexible, integrated, durable) and an **obsessive** one (rigid, conflicted, burnout-prone) is set by *how* the activity is internalized: autonomy support from the adults around a child pushes toward harmonious passion, while pressured specialization, over-identification, and **parents who over-value the activity** push toward obsessive passion (Mageau et al., 2009). Since control is precisely what spikes as stakes rise, the discipline is **counter-cyclical**: as the stakes go up, deliberately dial autonomy support *up* — more choice, more voice, more rationale, and an explicit decoupling of the child's worth from the outcome.
- **Consensus it breaks:** when it gets serious, take the wheel and apply pressure.
- **Backing:** Mageau et al. (2009), autonomy support → harmonious, parental valuation/pressured specialization → obsessive (samples include children and teenagers); Bartholomew et al. (2011), control → need thwarting → burnout; Assor, Roth & Deci (2004), conditional regard's affective costs. **Honest limit:** *structure* genuinely must rise with stakes (real deadlines exist); the claim is to raise structure and autonomy support while holding *control* and *contingent regard* down — not to abandon rigor.

### SPOV 4 — Never reward the return. Rewarding a passion is how you kill it, and it is worse in exactly the ages we serve.

- **Elaboration:** The obvious growth move is to incentivize practice: streaks, points, prizes, leaderboards. For an already-interesting activity this is self-sabotage. Expected, contingent tangible rewards reliably **undermine** intrinsic motivation, and the effect is **stronger in children than adults** (Deci, Koestner & Ryan, 1999). The classic demonstration is children who drew for an expected "good-player" award and then drew far less in free play (Lepper, Greene & Nisbett, 1973). The effect appears astonishingly early — 20-month-olds who got a material reward for helping *helped less* afterward, while those who got praise or nothing did not (Warneken & Tomasello, 2008). Because our core signal *is* voluntary return, gamifying it corrupts the very thing we measure. Note the boundary: **sincere, informational, process-focused acknowledgment is not a contingent tangible reward** — the Warneken result specifically spared the praise condition.
- **Consensus it breaks:** gamify engagement; reward practice to build the habit.
- **Backing:** Deci, Koestner & Ryan (1999), meta-analytic undermining, larger for children; Lepper et al. (1973); Warneken & Tomasello (2008). **Honest limit:** a competing tradition (Cameron & Pierce) argues undermining is narrow and that rewards can *start* a genuinely low-interest task; we design to the autonomy mechanism, and we allow informational feedback, not tangible contingent rewards on the passion signal.

### SPOV 5 — A missing week is a question about the environment, not a verdict on the child. Treat absence as a prompt to ask, never as an inference to act on.

- **Elaboration:** Engagement analytics treat declining logins as declining motivation and fire off nudges and "at-risk" flags. That is wrong twice over. First, youth disengagement/dropout is **multiply caused** — lack of enjoyment, competence worries, social pressures, competing priorities, and physical/maturational factors all drive it (Crane & Temple, 2015) — so a gap is genuinely ambiguous. Second, automated nudging *is* a controlling move, and inferring inner state from thin behavioral traces edges toward the affect-inference we have refused. So **missingness routes to a warm human check-in, never to an automated label or a nudge campaign.** And the inverse matters: **presence without depth** (showing up, going through the motions) is often a *worse* sign than a clean, declared break, because it is early devaluation wearing the costume of compliance.
- **Consensus it breaks:** falling usage = disengagement = intervene automatically.
- **Backing:** Crane & Temple (2015), five distinct dropout causes; Fredricks, Blumenfeld & Paris (2004), behavioral engagement is real but multi-determined; our locked refusal of affect detection. **Honest limit:** absence is not *noise* forever — a sustained, patterned decline across return **and** depth **and** devaluation is real signal; the discipline is about threshold and human interpretation, not ignoring the data.

### SPOV 6 — Warm-demanding beats "tiger" not because it demands less, but because the warmth is unconditional.

- **Elaboration:** The folk theory says strict-but-loving tiger parenting forges excellence. The longitudinal data say the opposite: across eight years of Chinese-American families, a **supportive** profile produced better GPA *and* better wellbeing than a **tiger** profile, which was associated with lower GPA, more academic pressure, more depressive symptoms, and more alienation (Kim et al., 2013). The active ingredient of the harm is **conditional regard** — making love, warmth, or approval contingent on performance — which produces compliance at a real price: introjected (internally pressured) motivation, fluctuating self-esteem, and resentment, *even when it "works"* behaviorally (Assor, Roth & Deci, 2004). The meta-analytic picture agrees: warmth, autonomy-granting, and authoritative parenting associate with better achievement, while harsh and psychological control associate with worse (Pinquart, 2016). So the demand is not the problem; **demand delivered through pressure and contingent worth is the problem.** Warm-demanding keeps expectations high and keeps regard unconditional.
- **Consensus it breaks:** high standards require pressure; a little contingent approval is a healthy motivator.
- **Backing:** Kim et al. (2013), tiger < supportive on achievement *and* adjustment; Assor, Roth & Deci (2004), conditional regard's costs; Pinquart (2016), authoritative/warmth/autonomy positive, harsh/psychological control negative. **Honest limit:** parenting-style effect sizes on achievement are *small* (Pinquart), culture moderates the meaning of "control," and demandingness delivered as *structure* is beneficial — it is the contingency of regard, not the height of the bar, that does the damage.

## 3. DOK 3 — Insights (evidence → our framework)

Original conclusions that bridge the Knowledge Tree to the decision framework.

- **Insight 1 — Difficulty and pressure are orthogonal control knobs; treat them separately.** The single biggest design error would be to bundle "how hard" with "how much it counts." Learning wants high challenge (Insight 2); wellbeing wants low control (SPOV 1, 3). Our framework therefore always names *two* moves: a challenge move (push/hold/scaffold) and a pressure move (autonomy support up/down). "Back off" almost always means the pressure knob first.
- **Insight 2 — There is a measurable target for challenge: ~80–90% success.** The zone of proximal development (Vygotsky; operationalized as scaffolding by Wood, Bruner & Ross, 1976) says aim just past independent ability. Wilson et al. (2019) put a number on the sweet spot — optimal training error ≈ 15.87%, i.e. ~85% success — for gradient-descent learners. We adopt **~80–90% success** as the difficulty setpoint, with the honest caveat that the precise figure comes from simple machine/animal learning and is a heuristic, not a law, for rich human skills.
- **Insight 3 — Optimal challenge is not one-size-fits-all; let the child weight it.** Challenge–skill balance predicts flow only *partially*, and is moderated by how much the person cares about the activity and by their achievement motive (Engeser & Rheinberg, 2008 — adult samples, flagged). Practical consequence: difficulty should be **co-set with the child**, not imposed, which also happens to be an autonomy-support move.
- **Insight 4 — Read burnout as the ABQ triad expressed in behavior.** Exhaustion, reduced accomplishment, and devaluation (Raedeke & Smith, 2001) each have behavioral fingerprints we can watch without faces: exhaustion → shorter sessions, longer latency to start, schedule/sleep encroachment, "playing hurt"; reduced accomplishment → retreating to easy tasks, abandoning attempts, hiding work; devaluation → declining voluntary return, compliance-without-depth, no self-initiated projects. **Devaluation is the highest-value, most GT100K-native marker** (SPOV 2).
- **Insight 5 — The harmonious→obsessive tip has named antecedents we can monitor and design against.** Over-identification (activity = whole self), pressured specialization, and parental over-valuation push toward obsessive passion (Mageau et al., 2009). This is *why* the pipeline keeps spikes plural and reversible, and it gives the mentor a concrete watchlist: single-identity language, inability to take a day off, guilt when not practicing, playing through injury/fatigue.
- **Insight 6 — Rising stakes is the trigger to add autonomy support, because that is when adults default to control.** The most dangerous moment for a deepening passion is the one that feels most like it justifies pressure (SPOV 3). Bake a counter-cyclical rule into the system: any stakes increase (competition, audience, deadline, specialization step) automatically pairs with an autonomy-support increase and a *reduction* in evaluative surfacing.
- **Insight 7 — The signal we prize is the signal a reward would poison.** Because voluntary return is intrinsic-motivation-dependent and undermining is worse in children (Deci, Koestner & Ryan, 1999; Warneken & Tomasello, 2008), the product must **never** attach streaks, points, or prizes to return. Feedback stays informational and process-focused; warmth stays non-contingent.
- **Insight 8 — Missingness is ambiguous by nature, so it must route to humans, not automations.** Dropout has at least five distinct causes (Crane & Temple, 2015). A gap therefore cannot be auto-interpreted; it triggers a curious human check-in. Distinguish a *single gap* (noise) from a *sustained multi-signal decline* (signal), and treat *presence-without-depth* as more concerning than a clean break (SPOV 5).
- **Insight 9 — The system proposes; the human disposes — especially for back-off and rest.** The system's job is to calibrate difficulty, surface behavioral patterns, and refuse to gamify. The *interpretation* of strain, and the decisions to back off or prescribe rest, belong to the mentor, because they require reading context (life events, family pressure, identity) the system must not infer. No automated "this child is burning out" label ever reaches the child.
- **Insight 10 — Rest and switching are healthy outcomes, not failures.** Because interest is *developed* and spikes are reversible, a prescribed deload, a parked spike, or a switch to a plural interest is a legitimate, even protective, result — consistent with keeping passion harmonious rather than obsessive. Rest is scheduled proactively (deload cycles), framed as reversible, and never charged with guilt.

## 4. DOK 2 — Knowledge Tree

DOK-1 facts are drawn from the source; DOK-2 summaries are in our words. All citations verified; links resolve to the record of version.

### Category A — Optimal challenge, flow, and the zone of proximal development

- **Source: Wood, Bruner & Ross (1976), "The Role of Tutoring in Problem Solving"**
  - **DOK 1 — Facts:** Coined "scaffolding" — a tutor controls the parts of a task beyond the learner so the child can work only on the parts within reach, studied with **3-, 4-, and 5-year-olds** building a pyramid. Names six scaffolding functions, including recruitment, reduction in degrees of freedom, **direction maintenance**, marking critical features, **frustration control**, and demonstration. A precondition: the learner must be able to *recognize* a correct solution before producing it unaided.
  - **DOK 2 — Summary:** The operational form of Vygotsky's ZPD for young children: aim just past independent ability and fade support as competence grows. "Frustration control" is an explicit tutor job — evidence that managing (not eliminating) difficulty is part of good instruction.
  - **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1469-7610.1976.tb00381.x

- **Source: Wilson, Shenhav, Straccia & Cohen (2019), "The Eighty Five Percent Rule for optimal learning"**
  - **DOK 1 — Facts:** For a broad class of gradient-descent learners on binary-classification tasks, the **optimal training error rate is ≈ 15.87% (≈ 85% accuracy)**; training at this difficulty yields exponentially faster learning than a fixed difficulty. Demonstrated in artificial neural nets and biologically plausible models of animal perceptual learning.
  - **DOK 2 — Summary:** A quantitative anchor for "optimal challenge": neither too easy nor too hard, around 85% success. We use ~80–90% as a difficulty setpoint. **Extrapolation flag:** derived from simple classification learning, not complex human skill acquisition; a heuristic, not a law.
  - **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-019-12552-4

- **Source: Engeser & Rheinberg (2008), "Flow, performance and moderators of challenge–skill balance"**
  - **DOK 1 — Facts:** Across three studies, the assumption that flow depends on challenge–skill balance was **only partially supported**; the relationship was **moderated by the perceived importance of the activity and the achievement motive**. Flow predicted performance in two of three studies (e.g., adding ~4% variance to a final-exam outcome).
  - **DOK 2 — Summary:** Optimal challenge is not universal — it depends on how much the person cares and their disposition. Difficulty should be personalized and co-set, not applied as a fixed formula. **Extrapolation flag:** university-student samples.
  - **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s11031-008-9102-4

### Category B — Motivation as stakes rise: autonomy support vs control, and reward-undermining

- **Source: Bartholomew, Ntoumanis, Ryan, Bosch & Thøgersen-Ntoumani (2011), "Self-Determination Theory and Diminished Functioning"**
  - **DOK 1 — Facts:** Three studies (incl. a daily diary) of athletes. Perceived **autonomy support** predicted need *satisfaction* → vitality/positive affect; perceived **coach control** better predicted need *thwarting* → maladaptive outcomes: **burnout, depression, disordered eating, negative affect, physical symptoms**, and even elevated physiological stress (secretory IgA) before training.
  - **DOK 2 — Summary:** Autonomy support and control are distinct pathways with distinct outputs; control uniquely drives ill-being via need thwarting. Backs the "cut pressure, keep challenge" and "counter-cyclical autonomy" rules. **Age note:** athlete samples skew older adolescent/adult; the control→thwarting→burnout chain replicates in adolescent soccer players 12–17 (e.g., Mars Aicart et al., 2017; González et al., 2016).
  - **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1177/0146167211413125

- **Source: Deci, Koestner & Ryan (1999), reward-undermining meta-analysis**
  - **DOK 1 — Facts:** Expected, tangible, contingent rewards undermine free-choice intrinsic motivation; the undermining is **stronger for children than college students**, while verbal praise helps children less than adults.
  - **DOK 2 — Summary:** Rewarding an already-interesting activity reduces later voluntary engagement — worse in our target ages. Gamifying voluntary return corrupts the exact signal we measure.
  - **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-2909.125.6.627

- **Source: Lepper, Greene & Nisbett (1973), "Undermining children's intrinsic interest with extrinsic reward"**
  - **DOK 1 — Facts:** Preschoolers with baseline interest in drawing were assigned to expected-award, unexpected-award, or no-award conditions. The **expected-award** group later showed **less** intrinsic interest (less free-play drawing, lower quality) than the other two.
  - **DOK 2 — Summary:** The original over-justification demonstration, in children: making an intrinsically interesting activity a means to a reward reduces later free engagement.
  - **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1037/h0035519

- **Source: Warneken & Tomasello (2008), "Extrinsic rewards undermine altruistic tendencies in 20-month-olds"**
  - **DOK 1 — Facts:** 20-month-olds given a **material reward** for helping subsequently helped **less** than infants given **social praise** or **no reward** (who did not decline).
  - **DOK 2 — Summary:** Over-justification appears at the very dawn of childhood, and — critically — **praise/informational acknowledgment did not undermine**. This draws the boundary: sincere, process-focused feedback is allowed; contingent tangible rewards are not.
  - **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1037/a0013860

### Category C — Passion type: harmonious vs obsessive and its antecedents

- **Source: Vallerand et al. (2003), "Les passions de l'âme: On obsessive and harmonious passion" (Dualistic Model of Passion)**
  - **DOK 1 — Facts:** Two passions: **harmonious** (from autonomous internalization; activity in harmony with the self, flexible engagement) and **obsessive** (from controlled internalization; rigid, conflicted persistence). Harmonious associates with positive affect/adjustment; obsessive with conflict and negative outcomes.
  - **DOK 2 — Summary:** Whether a deep pursuit is healthy depends on *how* it is internalized, not how intense it is — intensity is not the enemy; control is.
  - **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1037/0022-3514.85.4.756

- **Source: Mageau, Vallerand, Charest, Salvy, Lacaille, Bouffard & Koestner (2009), "On the Development of Harmonious and Obsessive Passion"**
  - **DOK 1 — Facts:** Three studies, beginners to experts. **Children and teenagers whose environment supports autonomy develop harmonious passion**; those with high **activity specialization**, high **identification** (activity used for self-definition), and **parents who highly value the activity** are more likely to develop **obsessive** passion. Autonomous personality → harmonious; controlled personality → obsessive.
  - **DOK 2 — Summary:** The developmental fork has named, monitorable antecedents. Autonomy support and reversibility protect harmonious passion; pressured specialization, over-identification, and parental over-valuation tip it obsessive. Directly grounds the plural/reversible design and the mentor's obsessive-tip watchlist.
  - **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1467-6494.2009.00559.x

- **Source: Curran, Hill, Appleton, Vallerand & Standage (2015), "The psychology of passion: a meta-analytical review"**
  - **DOK 1 — Facts:** 94 studies, 1,308 effect sizes. **Harmonious passion** corresponds with positive outcomes (positive affect, flow, performance). **Obsessive passion** corresponds with **both** positive and **negative** outcomes (negative affect, rumination — alongside some vitality). Relations were **largely invariant across age and gender**.
  - **DOK 2 — Summary:** Meta-analytic confirmation that harmonious passion is broadly adaptive while obsessive passion carries a maladaptive tail — and that this pattern does not disappear in younger samples (supports applying the model in ages 6–14).
  - **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s11031-015-9503-0

### Category D — Burnout and dropout: behavioral early warning and the meaning of missingness

- **Source: Raedeke & Smith (2001), "Development and Preliminary Validation of an Athlete Burnout Measure" (ABQ)**
  - **DOK 1 — Facts:** Athlete burnout = three dimensions: **emotional/physical exhaustion**, **reduced sense of accomplishment**, **sport devaluation** (cynical/indifferent attitude). The 15-item ABQ correlates positively with stress, anxiety, and **amotivation**, and negatively with enjoyment, commitment, and intrinsic motivation. Validated across ages and sports (gold-standard measure).
  - **DOK 2 — Summary:** Gives us a three-part behavioral rubric. Devaluation — declining valuing of the activity — is the dimension most native to our voluntary-return signal and most readable without affect detection.
  - **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1123/jsep.23.4.281

- **Source: Isoard-Gautheur, Guillet-Descas & Gustafsson (2016), "Athlete Burnout and the Risk of Dropout Among Young Elite Handball Players"**
  - **DOK 1 — Facts:** 458 athletes aged **14–18**; ABQ profiles at Time 1 predicted **playing status six years later**. The **higher-burnout** profile was **~2.2–2.4× more likely to have dropped out** than low-burnout or high-exhaustion-only profiles.
  - **DOK 2 — Summary:** Burnout is prospectively linked to dropout in youth, and — notably — an *exhaustion-only* profile was less predictive than the *full* burnout profile (which includes devaluation/reduced accomplishment). Reinforces watching the whole triad, weighting devaluation.
  - **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1123/tsp.2014-0140

- **Source: Coakley (1992), "Burnout among Adolescent Athletes: A Personal Failure or Social Problem?"**
  - **DOK 1 — Facts:** From interviews with 15 burned-out adolescent athletes, argues burnout is rooted in the **social organization** of high-performance activity — specifically **constrained identity development and lack of meaningful control** — not individual stress-coping failure. Prevention requires changing the environment and relationships, not just teaching the kid to cope.
  - **DOK 2 — Summary:** Relocates the cause of burnout from the child to the system around the child (control, unidimensional identity). Backs environment-level guardrails (plural identity, autonomy) over "toughen up" interventions.
  - **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1123/ssj.9.3.271

- **Source: Crane & Temple (2015), "A systematic review of dropout from organized sport among children and youth"**
  - **DOK 1 — Facts:** 43 studies. Youth dropout is driven more by **intrapersonal and interpersonal** than structural constraints, clustering into five areas: **lack of enjoyment, perceptions of competence, social pressures, competing priorities, and physical factors (maturation/injury)**.
  - **DOK 2 — Summary:** A gap in participation is genuinely multi-causal, so absence cannot be auto-read as burnout or as lost passion. Missingness must route to a human check-in that distinguishes among these causes.
  - **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1177/1356336X14555294

- **Source: Fredricks, Blumenfeld & Paris (2004), "School Engagement" (reused from BrainLift 02/passion)**
  - **DOK 1 — Facts:** Engagement splits into behavioral, emotional, and cognitive components; the behavioral channel is observable from what a learner does.
  - **DOK 2 — Summary:** Justifies instrumenting behavior (return, depth, persistence) as a real, measurable signal — distinct from the facial-emotion inference we refuse — while remembering it is multi-determined.
  - **Link:** https://doi.org/10.3102/00346543074001059

### Category E — Mentor/parent pressure: warm-demanding vs controlling ("tiger"), and contingent self-worth

- **Source: Grolnick & Pomerantz (2009), "Issues and Challenges in Studying Parental Control: Toward a New Conceptualization"**
  - **DOK 1 — Facts:** Proposes that only parenting characterized by **pressure, intrusion, and domination** be called **control**; parenting characterized by **guidance, expectations, and consistent structure** should be called **structure**. Structure supports children's competence; control undermines autonomy.
  - **DOK 2 — Summary:** The conceptual key to "warm-demanding." Demandingness delivered as *structure* is beneficial; the same-looking demandingness delivered as *control* is harmful. Our framework raises structure with stakes while holding control down.
  - **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1750-8606.2009.00099.x

- **Source: Assor, Roth & Deci (2004), "The Emotional Costs of Parents' Conditional Regard"**
  - **DOK 1 — Facts:** Perceived parental **conditional regard** (approval/warmth contingent on meeting expectations — studied across academic, sport, prosocial, and emotion domains) predicted **introjected internalization** (internal compulsion), **fluctuating self-esteem**, perceived parental disapproval, and **resentment**; introjection mediated behavioral compliance. Conditional regard "can promote enactment of the desired behaviors but does so with significant affective costs," and transmits across generations.
  - **DOK 2 — Summary:** The mechanism behind tiger-parenting harm: it can produce output while damaging the child's self-worth and relationship. Warmth must stay **unconditional**. **Age note:** relies on college-age recall of childhood; the mechanism is about child-rearing but the direct 6–14 measurement is thin.
  - **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1111/j.0022-3506.2004.00256.x

- **Source: Kim, Wang, Orozco-Lapray, Shen & Murtuza (2013), "Does 'Tiger Parenting' Exist?"**
  - **DOK 1 — Facts:** 444 Chinese-American families, three waves over **eight years** (early adolescence → emerging adulthood). Four profiles: supportive, easygoing, tiger, harsh. **Supportive** produced the **best** outcomes; **tiger** was associated with **lower GPA and educational attainment, more academic pressure, more depressive symptoms, and greater alienation** than supportive. Tiger parenting was not even the most common profile.
  - **DOK 2 — Summary:** Direct longitudinal evidence that "strict-but-loving" tiger parenting underperforms supportive parenting on both achievement and wellbeing. The demand is not what helps; the support is.
  - **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1037/a0030612

- **Source: Pinquart (2016), "Associations of Parenting Styles and Dimensions with Academic Achievement: A Meta-Analysis"**
  - **DOK 1 — Facts:** 308 studies. **Warmth/responsiveness, behavioral control, autonomy granting, and authoritative** style → better achievement (small effects, and predicting change over time). **Harsh control (r ≈ −0.16), psychological control (r ≈ −0.11 to −0.14), authoritarian, permissive, and neglectful** → lower achievement. Parenting-style effects are smaller than school-specific involvement.
  - **DOK 2 — Summary:** Meta-analytic backing for warm + autonomy-granting + structured (authoritative) over harsh/psychologically controlling — with the honest caveat that style effects on achievement are *small*.
  - **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s10648-015-9338-y

## 6. Recommendation for GT100K — the signal → action framework and burnout guardrails

### 6.1 Two knobs, always named separately

Every recommendation moves one or both of two independent knobs (Insight 1):

- **Challenge knob** — task difficulty relative to skill. Target the **~80–90% success "stretch zone"** (Insight 2), co-set with the child (Insight 3). Moves: **PUSH** (raise difficulty / fade scaffold / add a stretch or real-audience milestone) · **HOLD** (consolidate at current difficulty, vary reps) · **SCAFFOLD** (lower difficulty / add support).
- **Pressure knob** — stakes, evaluation, deadlines, contingency of regard, surveillance. Moves: **autonomy support UP** (more choice, voice, rationale; decouple worth from outcome) or **DOWN** (add real-world structure — never contingent regard). Default posture: **low control, high structure, high warmth.**

"Back off" = **pressure knob down first**; only reduce challenge if success has genuinely fallen out of the zone. "Rest" = a *prescribed*, guilt-free, reversible deload.

### 6.2 The decision table (behavioral signals → action)

All signals are behavioral; **no facial/affect detection**. "Return" = voluntary, depth-weighted re-engagement. "Depth" = per-session depth + self-initiated extension. "Success" = task success rate (challenge–skill fit).

| # | Behavioral state (what the traces show) | Read | System action | Mentor action |
|---|------------------------------------------|------|---------------|----------------|
| 1 | Return strong & **rising**; depth rising; success **>90%**; child voluntarily picks harder options | Under-challenged / ready | **PUSH** challenge: raise difficulty toward the zone, fade scaffolds, *offer* (never force) a harder optional milestone | Offer a stretch project / real audience; co-set a bigger goal; name the growth; keep the choice the child's |
| 2 | Return stable; depth stable; success **80–90%**; no strain signals | In the zone (flow-friendly) | **HOLD**: keep difficulty, vary tasks, continue gradual scaffold fade | Light touch; affirm process; **resist adding stakes**; protect autonomy |
| 3 | Success **<70%**; rising give-ups/help-requests; return dipping; **no** devaluation | Over-challenged / frustration | **SCAFFOLD**: lower difficulty into the zone, break into smaller wins, restore support | Normalize struggle (productive-failure framing); rebuild competence; reassure that "hard" ≠ "not yours" |
| 4 | An external-stakes event appears (competition, deadline, audience, specialization step, or a **parental valuation spike**) | Danger window (control reflex) | **Counter-cyclical autonomy**: hold challenge, **do not** add streaks/leaderboards/urgency, reduce evaluative surfacing | Autonomy support **up**: expand choice/voice/rationale; explicitly state regard is unconditional; keep the spike reversible |
| 5 | Exhaustion proxies **emerging** (shorter sessions, longer latency-to-start, schedule/sleep encroachment) **+** depth falling **+** return declining | Early burnout (exhaustion) | **BACK OFF**: pressure down, load down, pause any intensity ramp; **surface the pattern to the mentor** (never label the child) | Warm check-in; cut load and pressure; restore autonomy + play; screen over-scheduling/sleep; protect a light week |
| 6 | **Devaluation present** (compliance-without-depth, cancels, "just tell me what to do," stopped sharing) **or** obsessive-tip signs (can't take a day off, guilt when not practicing, playing hurt, single-identity) **or** sustained multi-signal decline | Burnout / obsessive tip | **REST**: prescribe a deload, stop all nudges, hand to a human | Prescribe a **guilt-free, reversible break**; broaden identity (re-open plural spikes); work with family to remove valuation pressure; consider re-contracting or parking the pursuit |
| 7 | A **gap** in return (missingness) | Ambiguous — a question, not a verdict | **No** auto-nudge, **no** at-risk label; after a threshold, flag for a human to reach out | Curious check-in; distinguish life-event/competing-priority from devaluation; a clean, declared break can be healthy |

Notes on reading the table:
- **Weight devaluation over exhaustion.** An exhausted-but-engaged child (row 5) needs rest and load relief; a devaluing child (row 6) needs the pursuit itself renegotiated. Exhaustion-only profiles predict dropout far less than full-burnout profiles (Isoard-Gautheur et al., 2016).
- **Presence-without-depth is worse than a clean gap.** Row 6 can hide inside apparent compliance; row 7 is often benign. Do not reward mere attendance.
- **Push only from strength.** Row 1 requires *rising* return and stretch-seeking, not just high success — high success with flat/declining return means bored *or* quietly disengaging, so verify before pushing.

### 6.3 Burnout guardrails (design-level, non-negotiable)

1. **Never gamify the return.** No streaks, points, prizes, or leaderboards attached to voluntary return or practice — it undermines the intrinsic signal, worst in our ages (Deci, Koestner & Ryan, 1999; Lepper et al., 1973; Warneken & Tomasello, 2008). Informational, process-focused feedback only.
2. **Regard is unconditional.** Warmth and approval are never contingent on performance, in the product or in the family protocol (Assor, Roth & Deci, 2004). This is the line between warm-demanding and tiger.
3. **The system proposes; humans dispose.** The system calibrates difficulty and surfaces patterns; a mentor makes every back-off/rest/quit judgment. **No automated burnout label ever reaches the child** (Insight 9; Coakley, 1992).
4. **Counter-cyclical autonomy.** Any stakes increase auto-pairs with an autonomy-support increase and reduced evaluative surfacing (SPOV 3; Mageau et al., 2009; Bartholomew et al., 2011).
5. **Keep spikes plural and reversible; watch identity breadth.** Single-identity ("I am only X") is an obsessive-passion antecedent; maintain at least one live alternate pursuit and make parking/switching cost-free (Mageau et al., 2009).
6. **Prescribe rest proactively.** Build scheduled deload cycles; frame rest as reversible and expected, never as failure (Insight 10).
7. **Family protocol = warm-demanding.** Coach families toward high expectations delivered as **structure + unconditional warmth + autonomy**, and off valuation-loading and contingent regard (Kim et al., 2013; Grolnick & Pomerantz, 2009; Pinquart, 2016).
8. **Missingness is a question, not a verdict.** Never auto-infer state from gaps; route to a human (Crane & Temple, 2015). And, per locked policy, **no facial/affect/emotion detection** anywhere in this loop.
9. **Difficulty setpoint ~80–90% success, co-set with the child** (Wilson et al., 2019; Wood et al., 1976; Engeser & Rheinberg, 2008).

### 6.4 The one-sentence operating rule

**Push challenge whenever the child is returning, going deeper, and reaching for harder — and the instant strain shows, pull the pressure lever before the difficulty lever, watch for quiet devaluation as the first alarm, and let a human, not the system, decide when a break or a switch is the healthy answer.**

### Honest limits of this memo

- The strongest burnout early-warning and dropout evidence is **adolescent sport (≈12–18)**; the 6–9 band is under-studied, and we treat sport findings as the best available proxy, not proof.
- The 85% difficulty setpoint comes from **machine/animal learning**; it is a heuristic for human skill, to be tuned empirically per domain and child.
- Some flow and reward-undermining specifics come from **adult samples** (flagged); the passion, SDT, and reward-undermining *mechanisms*, however, replicate in children, and reward-undermining is *stronger* in children.
- Parenting-style effects on achievement are **small** (Pinquart, 2016); we lean on them for the *wellbeing* and *passion-type* case (where effects are clearer), not as a large achievement lever.
- All behavioral inference is probabilistic and multi-determined (Crane & Temple, 2015); the framework's safety comes from **routing interpretation to humans**, not from trusting any single trace.
