# GT100K BrainLift.2: Beyond the Floor, Building and Proving the Spike

## Owners

- Felipe Caicedo
- David Ordoñez


## Purpose

The purpose of this BrainLift is to redefine what happens *after* a student clears the academic floor (SAT 1570+, 5s on BC Calculus, Physics C, Literature, and Language). It is built on the belief that the scores are table stakes, necessary but indifferentiable. And that the two questions that decide an elite outcome are *what makes this student singular* and *how can we prove this to an external viewer*. GT100K answers both by helping a child develop a differentiating spike (measured by voluntary return, not locking them into a specific path) and prove that spike is their own work (a tamper-evident record of the process plus a short human defense), without pressure, burnout, surveillance, or accusation.

### In Scope

- Developing durable interest and a singular spike through repeated, varied work, tracked as a revisable hypothesis (the Passion Engine).
- Proving authorship and understanding from a project's process and a human defense rather than from the finished artifact (the Evidence Graph).
- Spiky points of view about motivation, specialization, provenance, and assessment that a standard AI model would not produce.
- Structured context (facts, sources, insights) to hand directly to an AI in a conversation.

### Out of Scope

- The academic floor itself: mastery pedagogy, tutoring, and acceleration. We assume the student has cleared it and do not re-litigate how.
- AI-detection and plagiarism-scoring as an integrity mechanism. We reject it on the evidence (see SPOV 4).
- The 100,000-learner operations machine, admissions selection, cohort/rivalry systems, and family-fidelity screening from the earlier GT100K scope.

## DOK 4: Spiky Points of View (SPOVs)

Each one breaks with the current consensus and holds up anyway. They group by pillar, with a unifying idea at the end.

### On finding the spike

- **Spiky POV 1: "Find your passion" is harmful advice; passions are built, not discovered.**
   - **Elaboration:** Society and every interest-inventory product tell a child their passion is a fixed thing waiting to be uncovered. That framing backfires. A *fixed* theory of interest narrows a child's openness to other domains and drops their resilience: when the work turns hard, the child reads the difficulty as proof "it was not their interest after all," and their interest falls (O'Keefe, Dweck & Walton, 2018). Passions grow through repeated engagement and struggle. 
   - **Consensus it breaks:** every child has a latent passion to be found by introspection or a quiz.
   - **Backing:** O'Keefe, Dweck & Walton (2018); Hidi & Renninger (2006).

- **Spiky POV 2: What a child says they love barely predicts what they return to; the trustworthy signal is unprompted return after the pressure lifts.**
   - **Elaboration:** A child's self-report correlates only about 0.4 with what they do. Voluntary re-engagement predicts the future far better, once you switch off novelty, praise, deadlines, and nudges. In-the-moment interest, not incoming interest, predicted a student's course choice seven semesters later (Harackiewicz et al., 2008), and "motivation to reengage" marks the interest phases most sharply (Boeder et al., 2021). We measure what a child returns to after the pressure lifts.
   - **Consensus it breaks:** ask the kid (or the parent) what they like, and build on it.
   - **Backing:** Harackiewicz et al. (2008); Boeder et al. (2021); Ryan & Deci (2000).

- **Spiky POV 3: Start exploring as early as possible and begin developing early, because time-to-develop is the scarce resource. Just keep the spike reversible and plural.**
   - **Elaboration:** Two defaults fail a talented child. The well-rounded-childhood default delays any deep pursuit until the child is "old enough to choose." The hothouse default locks a six-year-old into one lane for life. One doesn't let a child develop genuine expertise and become a master of the craft, the other leads to rapid burnout. So we open the Interest Lab as early as **6–8** to give a child the longest runway to find where they voluntarily return, and we start developing a validated spike early instead of waiting. An early start needs design, not delay, to stay safe. The spike stays **reversible**: a child can switch or park it whenever they want. The spike stays **plural**: a child can develop more than one. We do not assume expertise transfers on its own. Far transfer of trained skill is close to a myth (Sala & Gobet: effect sizes collapse to near-zero under proper controls), so a second spike is a second genuine pursuit, not a free bonus from the first. What does carry across is an abstract principle, and only when someone deliberately teaches the comparison (structured analogical comparison roughly doubles transfer, 48% vs 19%, and almost never happens on its own). The evidence usually thrown at "early specialization" argues against irreversible, single-domain lock-in, the obsessive kind of pursuit, not against an early start. We take the early start and engineer out the lock-in.
   - **Consensus it breaks:** you must choose between a protected well-rounded childhood and early hothousing, and early depth means foreclosure.
   - **Backing:** Vallerand et al. (2003) and Mageau et al. (2009): reversibility and autonomy support separate durable *harmonious* passion from obsessive burnout. O'Keefe, Dweck & Walton (2018): a growth frame keeps a pursuit resilient and switchable. Credé, Tynan & Harms (2017): "consistency of interest" is the wrong demand for exploring children, so plurality is healthy. Frontiers (2023) and Macnamara et al. (2014): the late-specialization evidence warns against lock-in, not against an early start. Sala & Gobet (2017) and Barnett & Ceci (2002): far transfer of skill largely fails, so plurality means multiple genuine pursuits, not automatic cross-domain compounding. Gentner, Loewenstein & Thompson (2003): abstract principles transfer through deliberate comparison, not accumulated expertise.

### On proving the spike

- **Spiky POV 4: An AI-detection score must never touch a child.**
   - **Elaboration:** The industry aims a detector at the finished file and calls it integrity. The detector is noise. One study showed that fourteen of them are "neither accurate nor reliable" (Weber-Wulff 2023); a paraphrase drops the best to 4.6% (Krishna 2023); they false-flag non-native English at 61.3% (Liang 2023); OpenAI shut down its own at 26% accuracy; Vanderbilt turned Turnitin's off. A 1% error rate still wrongly accuses hundreds of children per school each year, and it hits non-native, neurodivergent, and minority students hardest. We never run a detector against a student.
   - **Consensus it breaks:** AI/plagiarism detection is the responsible default.
   - **Backing:** Weber-Wulff et al. (2023); Krishna et al. (2023); Liang et al. (2023); Vanderbilt (2023).

- **Spiky POV 5: You cannot prove authorship from the file, only from its process and a five-minute conversation.**
   - **Elaboration:** Everyone tries to solve authorship at the file: a detector, a watermark, a content credential, a signature. None of them can, because none speaks to who made it. A signature proves key-control and integrity, "not that physical interaction produced those states" (arXiv 2510.04964). C2PA "does not attest human authorship" by its own spec. Strong watermarking is *formally impossible* (Zhang, ICML 2024). Even a perfect tamper-evident record proves only that the story was not edited after sealing. Trust comes from outside the file, in named parties and in the student explaining their own work. So we prove authorship with the two things a file cannot fake. The first is the **process**: a tamper-evident graph of the real path, dead ends and declared AI help included. The second is a sampled **five-minute human defense**, the one check that survives AI and  one an in-person academy can easily run. Two claims about the process carry this. First, **a process is harder to fake than an artifact.** For example, AI can create a website in one step. Fabricating a convincing multi-day, multi-party trail of plans, failed attempts, revisions, and declared help takes real work, often as much as doing the project. Harder does not mean impossible: a determined student can hand-transcribe AI text to manufacture a plausible revision history, and provenance metadata can be stripped, which is why the process never stands alone and the human defense closes the gap.
   
    
    The strongest authorship signal in the literature is keystroke logging (a random forest separates authentic writing from transcription at about 99%; Trezise 2019; EDM 2024), and we are actively weighing whether to use it: it buys real discriminative power, but logging a child's keystrokes carries a heavy privacy cost, so we are leaving that question open for now rather than settling it. Second, **a process shows far more of the student than the product does.** The artifact is one snapshot of what got finished. The path exposes perseverance, resourcefulness, recovery after failure, and how a student reasons, the facets of ability a polished result erases. The finished product tells you the least about a learner. The process is where the learning and the ability live. (One honest caveat: oral-assessment evidence is mostly higher-ed and under-studied for ages 8–14. However, making sure we design against anxiety and have multiple reviewers, we can mitigate some of the concerns.)
   - **Consensus it breaks:** the world judges authorship and ability from the finished artifact, authorship by forensics and ability by polish.
   - **Backing:** arXiv 2510.04964; C2PA 2.4 spec; Zhang et al. (2024, ICML); Sotiriadou et al. (2020); Dawson two-lane model; TEQSA (2023); Trezise et al. (2019) and Horváth (2026) on process features improving authorship discrimination; QAA contract-cheating guidance.

### The unifying idea

- **Spiky POV 6: Honesty is the architecture. Every decision in the process has an accountable name behind it.**
   - **Elaboration:** The record ties every meaningful step, judgment, and piece of help to a specific actor who stands behind it: the student who authored an attempt, the AI tool that assisted (declared, as neutral provenance), the teacher who reviewed the work. Nothing in the process floats free of an owner. Each node carries its actor, so for any decision in a project you can ask "who is accountable for this?" and get a name. A human owns every grade. Failed attempts are first-class, and the graph keeps them with no silent deletions; we treat them as proof of process, not as failures to hide or punish. An interest hypothesis names the guide behind each revision and records the child's own position on it. This is accountability built into the structure.

## DOK 3: Insights

Original conclusions we reached after processing the sources. These are the bridge from the Knowledge Tree to the SPOVs.

### Finding the spike

- **Insight 1:** A child's stated interest is a weak signal; the reliable one is voluntary return after external pressure is removed. Self-report and behavior converge only ~0.4, and in-the-moment interest beat incoming interest at predicting real re-engagement years out. This is why the product's central measurement is delayed voluntary return, not a rating. (→ SPOV 2)
- **Insight 2:** Interest is a developmental trajectory, so it can be *engineered* by repeated, varied exposure rather than waited for. The four-phase model plus "develop your passion" evidence means we can manufacture the conditions for interest instead of hoping a child arrives with one. (→ SPOV 1)
- **Insight 3:** Because a fixed theory of interest makes children quit at the first difficulty, the product must never assign a fixed label and must frame every interest as buildable and revisable. The interface language ("current evidence suggests," "next test") is a direct consequence. (→ SPOV 1)
- **Insight 4:** Extrinsic rewards corrupt the very interest we are trying to measure, and worse in children than adults, so gamified engagement is self-defeating. We meter pressure with a budget instead of maximizing time-in-product. (→ SPOV 2, guardrails)
- **Insight 5:** The harm attributed to "early specialization" is really the harm of *irreversible, controlled* commitment (obsessive passion). Separate the early start from the lock-in, keep the spike reversible and plural, and you capture the runway without the burnout. (→ SPOV 3)
- **Insight 6:** Interest cannot be read from a face, voice, or gaze; only voluntary behavior counts. This rules out affect/"engagement" detection on both scientific and legal grounds and forces a behavior-only signal model. (→ guardrails)

### Proving the spike

- **Insight 7:** No artifact-level test (detector, watermark, signature) can establish human authorship, because authorship is a property of a *process and an understanding*, not of the finished bytes. Every artifact-level "solution" is category error. (→ SPOV 4/5)
- **Insight 8:** A process is both harder to fake and richer in signal than a product, though neither claim is absolute. Faking a multi-day, multi-party lineage is close to doing the work, but a determined student can still hand-transcribe or strip provenance, so the process is paired with a human defense. The lineage exposes perseverance, resourcefulness, and recovery that a polished result erases, but portfolio and process scores track task and context (~49%) far more than stable ability (~11%), so process is strong evidence of authorship and effort and only suggestive about ability. (→ SPOV 5)
- **Insight 9:** Cryptographic integrity is not authenticity. A self-consistent graph can still be fabricated, so trust must root outside the packet, in named signing parties and, above all, in a human defense. The trust roots (signed issuance → transparency log → per-actor signing) are a roadmap, not a finished guarantee. (→ SPOV 5/6)
- **Insight 10:** An in-person academy has a structural moat for authorship: it can run the five-minute human defense that remote, artifact-only products cannot. What competitors treat as an unscalable cost is our advantage. (→ SPOV 5)
- **Insight 11:** Accountability is stronger when it is structural than when it is policy. If every node carries its actor and the system refuses to assemble a record without a human owner for each judgment, honesty is enforced by construction rather than by rule. (→ SPOV 6)

### Composition and honest limits

- **Insight 12:** The two pillars are one machine. The Passion Engine generates the singular work; the Evidence Graph makes it believable. An unprovable spike is a claim, and a provenance system with nothing singular to prove is plumbing. The project is the seam that joins them.
- **Insight 13:** The core theories replicate in ages 6–14, so this is replication with refinements, not blind extrapolation. Self-determination need-satisfaction predicts motivation trajectories at 11–16 (Gnambs & Hanfstingl 2015); the four-phase model's situational/individual distinction holds at 12–14 (individual→situational .61); harmonious vs. obsessive passion replicates to mean age ~11; and reward-undermining is stronger in children than adults. Refinements to respect: academic intrinsic motivation declines across childhood, steepest in math; at ages 7–10 achievement predicts later motivation more than the reverse (Garon-Carrier 2016); and verbal praise helps children less than adults.
- **Insight 14:** Verifiable deletion on an append-only store, applied to child data, has no off-the-shelf answer. It is the hardest gate and must be solved before any live child enrolls.
- **Insight 15:** The two riskiest bets are demand-side pull (getting one gatekeeper to *require* a packet) and capture friction (evidence as a free byproduct of tools a kid already uses). Everything else is downstream of those two.
- **Insight 16:** Turning a validated interest into expertise is a staged, domain-specific process (Talent-Development Megamodel: potential → competency → expertise → eminence), and Renzulli's Enrichment-Triad Type III (a self-directed authentic project for a real audience, teacher as mentor) is that pathway made concrete. Our project spine has a name and an evidence base. (→ SPOV 3)
- **Insight 17:** Expertise does not transfer far on its own, so a "plural" strategy is not cross-subsidized by transfer: a second spike is a second real pursuit, and the only reliable cross-domain lever is deliberately teaching abstract comparison. This corrects an earlier overclaim that depth "compounds" across domains. (→ SPOV 3)
- **Insight 18:** Multipotentiality is mostly a measurement artifact: above-level testing reveals a differentiated profile in >95% of top-1% youth (SMPY), so most children have a genuine strongest domain to find, which supports the "find the spike" premise while cautioning that "equal potential everywhere" is a testing-ceiling illusion. Pursuing multiple genuine interests was not linked to career indecision, so plural is not diluting. (→ SPOV 1/2/3)

## DOK 2: Knowledge Tree

Organized sources and the facts and summaries we built our insights on. DOK 1 facts are raw from the source; DOK 2 summaries are in our own words.

### Category 1: Interest and passion development

#### Subcategory 1.1: How interest forms

- **Source: Hidi & Renninger (2006), "The Four-Phase Model of Interest Development"**
   - **DOK 1 - Facts:**
      - Interest is defined as "the predisposition to reengage with particular classes of objects, events, or ideas over time."
      - The four phases are triggered situational, maintained situational, emerging individual, and well-developed individual interest.
      - The first two phases depend on external support; the later two are increasingly self-generated.
   - **DOK 2 - Summary:**
      - Interest is a trajectory, not a trait, and the marker of progress is whether a learner re-engages without external support.
      - Because early phases need scaffolding, a program can deliberately trigger and maintain interest rather than wait for it.
   - **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1207/s15326985ep4102_4

- **Source: Harackiewicz et al. (2008), "Interest and course choice"**
   - **DOK 1 - Facts:**
      - Situational interest generated *during* a course, independent of incoming interest, predicted later free-choice re-engagement (enrollment/major up to seven semesters later, N = 858).
      - Incoming (pre-existing) self-reported interest was the weaker predictor.
   - **DOK 2 - Summary:**
      - What a student experiences while doing the work predicts their future better than what they said they liked beforehand.
      - This is the empirical basis for measuring in-the-moment and delayed return over intake surveys.
   - **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1037/0022-0663.100.1.105

- **Source: Boeder et al. (2021), "Interest Development Scale," Motivation Science**
   - **DOK 1 - Facts:**
      - The IDS has five factors (information seeking, motivation to reengage, persistence, self-regulation, value).
      - "Motivation to reengage" was the factor that discriminated among interest phases most sharply.
      - The scale was developed with adult samples, not children.
   - **DOK 2 - Summary:**
      - Re-engagement is the single best measurable marker of where a learner sits on the interest trajectory.
      - The child-validation gap is a real limitation for our 6–14 target.
   - **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1037/mot0000204

#### Subcategory 1.2: Intrinsic motivation and its fragility

- **Source: Ryan & Deci (2000) / Deci & Ryan (2000), Self-Determination Theory**
   - **DOK 1 - Facts:**
      - Autonomy, competence, and relatedness are the three basic needs supporting internalized motivation.
      - Externally regulated behavior "is unlikely to continue when the external contingencies are removed."
      - The autonomous-versus-controlled distinction predicts sustained behavior better than intrinsic-versus-extrinsic.
   - **DOK 2 - Summary:**
      - Motivation that depends on external pressure evaporates when the pressure stops, so durable interest must be autonomous.
      - Any intervention that raises output while harming autonomy or belonging is a harmful dose, not a win.
   - **Link:** https://selfdeterminationtheory.org/

- **Source: Deci, Koestner & Ryan (1999), reward-undermining meta-analysis; Lepper, Greene & Nisbett (1973), over-justification**
   - **DOK 1 - Facts:**
      - Expected, contingent tangible rewards undermined free-choice intrinsic motivation (engagement d = −0.40, completion −0.36, performance −0.28).
      - Tangible rewards were more detrimental for children than for college students.
      - In the "magic markers" study, children who expected a reward for drawing later drew about half as much in free play.
      - The undermining is stronger in children than college students (tangible d = −0.39 vs −0.27; engagement-contingent −0.46 vs −0.21, Qb = 6.76, p < .01), while verbal praise helps children less (d = 0.11, ns, vs 0.43 for adults).
   - **DOK 2 - Summary:**
      - Rewarding an already-interesting activity reliably reduces later voluntary engagement in it, and the effect is worse for children, exactly our target band.
      - Gamified engagement therefore corrupts the exact signal (voluntary return) the Passion Engine measures.
      - Conflict to flag: a competing meta-analytic tradition (Cameron & Pierce) argues reward-undermining is narrow and non-universal, and rewards can raise motivation for low-interest tasks; we design to the autonomous-vs-controlled mechanism rather than to a single contested effect size.
   - **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-2909.125.6.627

#### Subcategory 1.3: Passion, specialization, and burnout

- **Source: Vallerand et al. (2003), Dualistic Model of Passion; Mageau et al. (2009)**
   - **DOK 1 - Facts:**
      - Harmonious passion comes from autonomous internalization; obsessive passion from controlled internalization.
      - Harmonious passion is associated with positive affect and flexible engagement; obsessive passion with rigid persistence, conflict, and burnout.
      - In children/adolescents, autonomy support from parents and teachers predicted harmonious passion and negatively predicted obsessive passion.
   - **DOK 2 - Summary:**
      - Whether a pursuit is healthy depends on *how* it is internalized, not how intense it is.
      - Autonomy support and the freedom to disengage are what keep an early, deep pursuit from tipping into burnout.
   - **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1037/0022-3514.85.4.756

- **Source: Frontiers in Sports & Active Living (2023), early- vs. late-specialization review; Macnamara et al. (2014); Credé et al. (2017)**
   - **DOK 1 - Facts:**
      - "Predictors of rapid junior performance and of long-term senior performance are opposite"; most elite athletes specialize in mid-to-late adolescence.
      - Deliberate practice explained about 4% of performance variance in education and less than 1% in professions.
      - Grit's "consistency of interest" facet is weak and strongly overlaps conscientiousness.
   - **DOK 2 - Summary:**
      - Early single-domain lock-in is neither necessary for elite outcomes nor safe, and demanding "consistency of interest" is wrong for exploring children.
      - This supports an early start that stays plural and reversible rather than committed and singular.
   - **Link:** https://doi.org/10.3389/fspor.2023.1175718

#### Subcategory 1.4: Measurement and affect

- **Source: Barrett et al. (2019), "Emotional expressions reconsidered"; EU AI Act, Article 5(1)(f) / Recital 44**
   - **DOK 1 - Facts:**
      - Emotion "cannot be reliably read from facial configurations"; "variability is the norm."
      - Automated engagement detectors degrade out-of-sample (reported ~95.7% train to ~76% test).
      - The EU AI Act prohibits emotion inference in education for "lack of scientific basis" (in force Feb 2, 2025).
   - **DOK 2 - Summary:**
      - Reading interest or engagement off a face is scientifically unfounded and, in the EU, illegal in schools.
      - The signal model must rely on voluntary behavior only.
   - **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1177/1529100619832930

#### Subcategory 1.5: Interest and motivation specifically in ages 6–14

- **Source: Gnambs & Hanfstingl (2015); Gillet, Vallerand & Lafrenière (2012); Garon-Carrier et al. (2016); grade 7–8 interest study (2021)**
   - **DOK 1 - Facts:**
      - SDT need-satisfaction (autonomy, competence, relatedness) predicts intrinsic-motivation trajectories in 11–16-year-olds; controlling need satisfaction flattens the decline (Gnambs & Hanfstingl 2015).
      - Academic intrinsic motivation declines from ~age 9–12, stabilizes to ~15, then rises, with the steepest decline in mathematics (Gillet et al. 2012).
      - In grades 1–4 (ages 7–10), prior achievement predicts later intrinsic motivation, but motivation does not predict later achievement (Garon-Carrier et al. 2016).
      - The four-phase model's individual→situational-interest link holds at ages 12–14 (standardized effect 0.61), but only triggered situational interest scaffolds learning gains in this band.
   - **DOK 2 - Summary:**
      - The motivation theories replicate in ages 6–14 with refinements, so extrapolating from older samples is far less of a leap than first flagged.
      - Design implications: expect a natural motivation dip in early adolescence, build competence first (achievement feeds motivation in the youngest band), and lean on triggered situational interest.
   - **Link:** https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11218-011-9170-2 ; https://selfdeterminationtheory.org/wp-content/uploads/2016/11/2016_Garon-Carrier_etal_Child_Development.pdf ; https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8386765/

### Category 2: Authorship and provenance

#### Subcategory 2.1: Why AI-detection fails

- **Source: Weber-Wulff et al. (2023); Krishna et al. (2023); Liang et al. (2023); Vanderbilt (2023)**
   - **DOK 1 - Facts:**
      - Fourteen detectors tested across 754 cases were "neither accurate nor reliable"; none exceeded 80% accuracy.
      - A paraphraser dropped DetectGPT from 70.3% to 4.6% accuracy at a fixed 1% false-positive rate.
      - Seven GPT detectors misclassified non-native (TOEFL) English essays as AI at an average 61.3% false-positive rate.
      - OpenAI discontinued its own classifier (26% true-positive, 9% false-positive at launch); Vanderbilt disabled Turnitin's AI detector in Aug 2023.
   - **DOK 2 - Summary:**
      - AI-text detection is inaccurate, trivially defeated, and biased against the most vulnerable students.
      - Any nonzero error rate produces large numbers of wrongful accusations at school scale, so we never aim one at a child.
   - **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s40979-023-00146-z

#### Subcategory 2.2: What cryptography can and cannot prove

- **Source: Proof-of-process (arXiv 2510.04964); C2PA Specification 2.4; Zhang et al. (2024, ICML), "Watermarks in the sand"**
   - **DOK 1 - Facts:**
      - A signature "does not prove she wrote it… a valid signature chain over document hashes demonstrates commitment to each state, not that physical interaction produced those states."
      - C2PA 2.4 "makes no judgment about whether provenance is 'true'" and "does not attest human authorship."
      - Strong watermarking of generative models is impossible under natural assumptions; a bounded attacker can erase any watermark without significant quality loss.
   - **DOK 2 - Summary:**
      - Cryptographic tooling proves key-control, integrity, and timing, never that a human authored the content unaided.
      - So C2PA is export-only and watermarks are not the truth layer; authorship rests on process plus a human defense.
   - **Link:** https://arxiv.org/pdf/2510.04964

#### Subcategory 2.3: Verifying understanding (oral defense)

- **Source: Sotiriadou et al. (2020); Dawson two-lane model; TEQSA (2023)**
   - **DOK 1 - Facts:**
      - Interactive oral assessment "demonstrated resilience to academic misconduct, including inappropriate use of Generative AI."
      - Dawson's two essentials for secured assessment are authentication and control of circumstances.
      - TEQSA: "trustworthy judgements about student learning require multiple, inclusive and contextualised approaches."
      - Most rigorous oral-assessment evidence is from higher education, not ages 8–14.
   - **DOK 2 - Summary:**
      - A live, dialogic defense is the strongest single check on understanding and resists AI misconduct.
      - It must be sampled across multiple touchpoints and designed for young learners, since the age evidence is thin.
   - **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1080/03075079.2019.1582015

#### Subcategory 2.4: Provenance machinery and erasure

- **Source: W3C PROV, in-toto, SLSA, Sigstore/Rekor; EDPB Guidelines 02/2025**
   - **DOK 1 - Facts:**
      - Content-addressing makes any one-bit change cascade through history; verification is O(log n) with no trusted third party.
      - in-toto binds signed metadata to artifacts by cryptographic digest; SLSA publishes a hash-of-attestation plus a transparency-log pointer.
      - EDPB (02/2025): technical immutability "cannot be invoked to justify non-compliance" with the right to erasure; crypto-shredding is a supplement, not a complete guarantee.
   - **DOK 2 - Summary:**
      - The integrity and attestation machinery already exists and is reusable; the novel work is applying it to children's project work.
      - Reconciling an append-only chain with a child's right to erasure is the genuinely unsolved problem.
   - **Link:** https://slsa.dev/spec/v1.0/about ; https://www.edpb.europa.eu/our-work-tools/documents/public-consultations/2025/guidelines-022025-processing-personal-data_en

### Category 3: Talent development (turning interest into expertise)

#### Subcategory 3.1: Frameworks and longitudinal evidence

- **Source: Subotnik, Olszewski-Kubilius & Worrell (2011/2021), Talent-Development Megamodel**
   - **DOK 1 - Facts:**
      - Talent development is domain-specific and stage-sequential: potential → competency → expertise → eminence.
      - It is driven by opportunities "inside, outside, and beyond school."
      - Malleable psychosocial skills and "insider knowledge" matter more at higher stages.
   - **DOK 2 - Summary:**
      - A validated interest becomes expertise through differentiated opportunity plus psychosocial-skill development across stages.
      - This is the backbone of our Specialization Planner.
   - **Link:** https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-030-56869-6_24

- **Source: Renzulli, Three-Ring Conception and the Enrichment Triad**
   - **DOK 1 - Facts:**
      - Creative-productive giftedness is the interaction of above-average ability (top 15–20% of a domain), task commitment ("fascination"), and creativity; no single ring suffices.
      - Enrichment Triad Type III is a self-selected authentic investigation of a real problem for a real audience.
      - The teacher shifts from instructor to "coach, mentor, guide-on-the-side."
   - **DOK 2 - Summary:**
      - Type III is almost exactly our project-spine-plus-guide model, and the ability bar (top 15–20% of a domain) is looser than an IQ cutoff.
   - **Link:** https://gifted.uconn.edu/schoolwide-enrichment-model/the-enrichment-triad-model/

- **Source: Study of Mathematically Precocious Youth (Lubinski & Benbow)**
   - **DOK 1 - Facts:**
      - A ~2-hour above-level test at age 12–13 predicts expert outcomes far above base rates (700+ on SAT-M/V → ~50% earn doctorates vs a ~1% base rate).
      - Ability tilt (pattern) channels which domain; ability level predicts how high one rises, even within the top 1%.
      - Pace-matched acceleration converts high ability into achievement without academic or social-emotional harm.
   - **DOK 2 - Summary:**
      - Find the domain from the ability tilt and develop it early with acceleration; above-level testing surfaces a differentiated profile in >95% of top-1% youth.
      - Tension to hold: Renzulli's IQ-120 "threshold" versus SMPY's finding that ability keeps mattering at the extreme.
   - **Link:** https://my.vanderbilt.edu/smpy/

### Category 4: Transfer of learning

#### Subcategory 4.1: Near vs. far transfer

- **Source: Barnett & Ceci (2002); Sala & Gobet (2017); Gentner, Loewenstein & Thompson (2003)**
   - **DOK 1 - Facts:**
      - Barnett & Ceci frame transfer as a multidimensional "distance" problem: near transfer (adjacent domains) is common, far transfer is contingent and largely unproven.
      - Sala & Gobet: across chess, music, and working-memory training, far-transfer effects shrink to near-zero once active/placebo controls and publication bias are handled (a second-order meta-analysis found g = 0.877 trained, 0.274 near, 0.121 far).
      - Gentner et al.: explicitly comparing two surface-different analogs roughly doubles transfer (48% vs 19%); learners almost never link them spontaneously (16% vs 98% when instructed to compare).
   - **DOK 2 - Summary:**
      - Depth in one domain does not automatically accelerate another, so a "plural" strategy is fine but is not cross-subsidized by transfer.
      - The one reliable lever is deliberately teaching abstract comparison across a child's domains.
   - **Link:** https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-2909.128.4.612 ; https://doi.org/10.1177/0963721417712760

### Category 5: Elite admissions (why a spike matters)

#### Subcategory 5.1: Angular vs. well-rounded

- **Source: College Board holistic-review guidance; CNBC (former Stanford AO, 2018); MIT and Caltech admissions guidance; Pioneer Academics activity survey (2025)**
   - **DOK 1 - Facts:**
      - College Board holistic-review guidance treats a "national science medal" and a migrant child's self-built "solar-powered chili roaster" as equivalent evidence of the same ability, and says the committee weighs "the range of criteria it needs in a class, not just in individual applicants."
      - A former Stanford AO: "We are not necessarily looking for all well-rounded students"; an "angular" applicant can be more compelling, and excellence in one area buys "the benefit of the doubt" elsewhere.
      - MIT advises depth over breadth ("quality over quantity"); Caltech openly rejects the "well-rounded" label for single-minded STEM focus.
      - A Pioneer Academics survey found elite admit rates peaked at ~4 thematically-related activities (30%) and fell with more (6 = 7%); admitted students averaged 3.7.
   - **DOK 2 - Summary:**
      - At the most selective tier, a deep differentiated spike beats an equally-credentialed well-rounded profile; the effect is tier-specific and a spike is not strictly required.
      - Value comes from the thoughtfulness and execution of a distinctive project, not its novelty. This is the evidence base for the North Star.
   - **Link:** https://highered.collegeboard.org/media/pdf/understanding-holistic-review-he-admissions.pdf
