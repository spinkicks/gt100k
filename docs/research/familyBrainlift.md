# Select the Family, Not the Child

**Owners:** David & Felipe

## Purpose

Choose whom to admit to GT 100k by choosing the family, not the child. The program runs an eight-year push toward MIT-level readiness, and the input that decides the result is the home a child comes from, not a six-year-old's test score. This brainlift makes the case for family selection and sets out what a hardened admissions process should screen for.

### In scope
- The family as the unit of admission, and the traits and commitments worth screening.
- The evidence behind each position: twin studies, the Polgar case, and SMPY.
- Selection principles a hardened admissions process can act on.

### Out of scope
- The child's capability assessment (CogAT, Track A/B). That runs through an external process (PRD §3.4) and is not ours to rebuild.
- Building the screening product or its instruments.
- Live-policy or legal claims for GT.

## DOK 4: Spiky POVs

### SPOV 1: Select on the family's commitment over the child's intake score.
The variance that predicts elite outcomes sits in whether a family will reorganize its life around the program, not in a six-year-old's test score. The twin-study result that shared environment barely moves adult IQ is a range-restriction artifact: no family in those samples ran an all-in home, so the data is blind to the environment that produces prodigies.

**Consensus it breaks:** admissions ranks children by early test scores and treats the home as noise.
**Backing:** Polderman et al. (2015); Bouchard & McGue (1981); the Polgar case.

### SPOV 2: Select for parental obsession, and screen for it on purpose.
The active ingredient is the parent's single-minded commitment, the same trait schools and social workers treat as a hazard. Target it. Screen the parents for grit, focus, and resistance to social pressure, and weight that above another round of testing on the child.

**Consensus it breaks:** policy treats intense parents as a risk to contain and screens the child, not the parent.
**Backing:** the Polgar case; SMPY commitment findings (Lubinski & Benbow).

### SPOV 3: Admit and bind the household, not the child.
The unit of selection is the household. Weight two-parent buy-in and enrolling siblings, and require a binding multi-year commitment that filters out families who quit when relatives and the school board push back.

**Consensus it breaks:** admissions admits one child at a time and treats the family as a signature, not the thing being selected.
**Backing:** the Polgar case, where one household reorganized around a single pursuit for a decade.

### SPOV 4: Ignore the "stolen childhood" objection; it isn't in the data.
The main charge against selecting fanatical families is that it steals childhoods. The long-term data on radical acceleration shows no harm to well-being. Treat a family's resolve against that objection as a positive signal.

**Consensus it breaks:** the "stolen childhood" worry is treated as a settled ethical limit.
**Backing:** Bernstein, Lubinski & Benbow (2021); the adult outcomes of the Polgar daughters.

## DOK 3: Insights

- **Heritability describes the sample, not the ceiling.** A twin study can only measure the homes its families ran, and none ran an all-in home. So "shared environment near zero" says nothing about the home that manufactures a prodigy. It marks that the home is missing from the data, not that the home does nothing. (Polderman; Bouchard & McGue)
- **The Polgar result reads as repeatable intent, not luck.** One family reorganized around one pursuit and produced three elite performers. Three hits from one household point at the household. The active variable is total parental commitment. (Polgar)
- **Commitment is the screenable predictor; the intake score is a floor check.** A merely able child in a committed home beats a gifted child in a lukewarm one. So the admissions ranking variable is the family's willingness to run the eight-year dose, and the child's score sets a floor rather than the order. (synthesis of Polderman, Robertson et al., Polgar)
- **The strongest ethical objection fails on its own evidence.** SMPY's 35-year data shows acceleration does not damage well-being, so the "stolen childhood" worry is not a reason to reject committed families. A family that holds its position against that pressure is showing the trait the program needs. (Bernstein, Lubinski & Benbow)
- **A hardened process scores the household.** The selection instrument reads two-parent buy-in, a structured low-distraction home, and a binding multi-year commitment. It uses the child's assessment only to clear a capability floor and puts the family's commitment at the center of the decision. (synthesis)

## DOK 2: Knowledge Tree

### Behavioral genetics and the limits of twin studies

**Polderman et al. (2015). Meta-analysis of the heritability of human traits based on fifty years of twin studies. *Nature Genetics*, 47, 702–709.**
- DOK 1 facts:
  - Pooled 2,748 publications covering 14,558,903 twin pairs across 17,804 traits.
  - Across traits, heritability estimates average about 49%.
  - For most trait domains, the shared-environment component is small once genetics is accounted for.
- DOK 2 summary: The largest twin meta-analysis to date finds a large genetic component and a small average shared-environment component across human traits. The field reads this as parenting barely moving the ceiling. The estimate only covers the range of homes present in the samples, and ordinary twin samples hold no all-in home, so the number bounds what average parenting does, not what a fanatical one could do.
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1038/ng.3285

**Bouchard & McGue (1981). Familial studies of intelligence: a review. *Science*, 212(4498), 1055–1059.**
- DOK 1 facts:
  - Reviewed 111 studies of IQ correlations among relatives.
  - Identical twins reared apart correlated about 0.72 on IQ.
  - Correlations rose with genetic relatedness across the reviewed designs.
- DOK 2 summary: A broad review of family IQ correlations showing that resemblance tracks genetic relatedness, with reared-apart identical twins still highly similar. It is a base for the claim that home environment adds little. As with Polderman, the reared-apart homes are ordinary adoptive homes, so the design never tests a home built to manufacture excellence.
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1126/science.7195071

### The engineered-prodigy case

**The Polgar family (László Polgar; Susan, Sofia, and Judit Polgar). *Raise a Genius!* (László Polgar).**
- DOK 1 facts:
  - László Polgar home-schooled his three daughters with chess as the central pursuit from early childhood.
  - Judit became a grandmaster at 15 years 4 months, breaking Fischer's youngest-GM record of the time, and reached world No. 8 overall.
  - Susan (Zsuzsa) was among the first women to earn the grandmaster title through standard tournament norms; Sofia earned the international master title.
- DOK 2 summary: A single family set out to produce chess prodigies and produced three, including the strongest female player in history. This is a case study, not a controlled trial, so it cannot prove causation on its own. It does show that the far-tail home twin studies never sample can produce repeated elite outcomes, which is the environment SPOV 1 through SPOV 3 want to select.
- Link: László Polgar, *Raise a Genius!* (English translation, 2017).

### SMPY: ability, commitment, and long-term well-being

**Bernstein, Lubinski & Benbow (2021). Academic acceleration in gifted youth and fruitless concerns regarding psychological well-being. *Journal of Educational Psychology*, 113(4), 830–845.**
- DOK 1 facts:
  - Drew on SMPY's longitudinal cohorts with follow-up over about 35 years.
  - Compared accelerated and non-accelerated high-ability students matched on ability and background.
  - Found no evidence that acceleration harmed later psychological well-being, alongside strong attainment.
- DOK 2 summary: Long-run SMPY data shows accelerated gifted students end up at least as well off psychologically as their non-accelerated peers, with strong careers and creative output. It removes the empirical ground under the "stolen childhood" objection and supports treating a family's willingness to accelerate as a positive signal.
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1037/edu0000500

**Robertson, Smeets, Lubinski & Benbow (2010). Beyond the threshold hypothesis. *Current Directions in Psychological Science*, 19(6), 346–351.**
- DOK 1 facts:
  - Tracked outcomes within the top 1% of ability.
  - More ability inside that top band still predicted more elite achievement.
  - Found no threshold above which added ability stopped mattering.
- DOK 2 summary: Ability keeps paying off even among the already-gifted, so a capability floor is real and worth setting. This supports using the child's score as a floor check while family commitment drives the ranking.
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1177/0963721410391442

### Bounding the "hours alone" view

**Macnamara, Hambrick & Oswald (2014). Deliberate practice and performance in music, games, sports, education, and professions: a meta-analysis. *Psychological Science*, 25(8), 1608–1618.**
- DOK 1 facts:
  - Meta-analysis across domains of the practice-to-performance link.
  - Deliberate practice explained about 4% of performance variance in education.
  - The share left to other factors was large, especially in academic domains.
- DOK 2 summary: Practice hours matter but explain a small slice of academic performance, so a program cannot buy elite outcomes with drill alone. Set next to the family-selection thesis, it points at the home that supplies the dose and the conditions, not the hours by themselves, as the lever.
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1177/0956797614535810
