# Select the Family, Not the Child

**Owners:** David & Felipe

## Purpose

Build a hardened process for choosing whom to admit to GT 100k, an eight-year push to MIT-level readiness (SAT 1570+ and AP 5s by age 14). The bet: rank on the family, not the child. Two hard floors gate the door, a cognitive floor on the child and the ability to carry the standard eight-year cost. Above the floors I rank families by their commitment to run the program. This brainlift argues that bet and sets the selection principles behind it.

### In scope
- The family as the ranking variable, and the traits, agreements, and commitments worth screening.
- The evidence for and against each position, stated honestly.
- Selection principles a hardened admissions process can act on.

### Out of scope
- The child's capability assessment itself. That runs through an external process (PRD §3.4) and is not ours to build.
- The screening product and its instruments.
- Live-policy or legal claims.

## DOK 4: Spiky POVs

### SPOV 1: I rank families, not children.
The child's intake score is a floor, and the standard eight-year cost is another floor. Above both, I rank on one thing: whether the family will run the program for eight years. Parental expectations predict not just where a child sits but how much they gain, over and above prior scores, so the family is the variable that moves the outcome and the six-year-old's test is a gate, not a ranking. The per-year effect is moderate. It compounds across eight years, and it is the only lever I can screen before a child ever enrolls.

**Consensus it breaks:** admissions ranks children by early test scores and treats the family as background.
**Backing:** Pinquart & Ebeling (2020); Hill & Tyson (2009); Fan & Chen (2001). Honest limit: this evidence is correlational with moderate effects, so I treat family commitment as the best available pre-enrollment signal, not a guarantee.

### SPOV 2: Twin studies can't see the home I select for.
"Shared environment barely moves IQ" holds only across ordinary homes. Polderman's own number is a 17% shared-environment share on average, not zero, and it climbs where environments actually differ: adopting a child out of a deprived home into a rich one moves measured IQ by double digits, and shared environment dominates in low-resource homes. No twin sample contains an all-in home that strips distraction and runs the dose for eight years, so the estimate is blind to it. I am not claiming I raise a child's adult IQ ceiling. I am claiming the all-in home converts a given ceiling into the SAT and AP achievement that ordinary homes leave on the table.

**Consensus it breaks:** the field reads "low shared-environment heritability" as "the home does not matter."
**Backing:** Polderman et al. (2015); Capron & Duyme (1989); Turkheimer et al. (2003). Honest limit: the strongest gene-by-SES effect is a US low-income finding, and the Wilson effect shows heritability rises with age, so I scope the claim to childhood achievement rather than the adult ceiling.

### SPOV 3: I recruit the intense parent the system treats as a problem, and I screen for the warm version of it.
The parent who pulled a kid out of a lazy classroom and reorganized the home is the signal, not the risk. The evidence sets a sharp condition: the cold, controlling "tiger" parent underperforms the warm, demanding one on the child's own grades, and achievement pressure does real damage when it makes a child's worth ride on performance. So I screen for sustained, warm, high-expectation commitment, and I measure the parent's conscientiousness rather than a "grit" score that is mostly conscientiousness relabeled. I test the parent, not the child again.

**Consensus it breaks:** schools and clinics treat intense parents as a hazard to manage.
**Backing:** Kim et al. (2013); Poropat (2009); Credé et al. (2017); Luthar et al. (2020). Honest limit: no one has validated a parent screen that predicts elite outcomes, so this is a selection bet, and I design against contingent-worth harm on purpose rather than pretending it away.

### SPOV 4: I admit the household whole, unanimous and bound.
Every adult in the home is in, whatever the number. Two parents means both; one parent means that one. This is not a two-parent rule. The two-parent advantage runs through resources and coordinated parenting, not the marriage certificate, and one adult who quietly disagrees is a standing conflict that predicts worse outcomes across eight years. So I require unanimity plus a binding multi-year commitment, because people who bind their future selves follow through, and the families who will not sign are the ones who fold when relatives and the school board start calling it a stolen childhood.

**Consensus it breaks:** admissions admits one child at a time and treats the family as a signature.
**Backing:** Teubert & Pinquart (2010); Amato (2005); Bryan, Karlan & Nelson (2010); Thaler & Benartzi (2004). Honest limit: commitment-device evidence is short-horizon and voluntary, so the eight-year family contract is an extrapolation I make on purpose. It doubles as a filter, since the families who refuse to sign select themselves out.

### SPOV 5: There is no stolen childhood, because the child owns the goal.
The real objection is not that the child ends up unhappy. The long-term acceleration data already answers that. The real objection is consent: a six-year-old cannot agree to eight years. My answer is that consent here is not a one-time signature but ongoing, revocable assent, and the home I select produces a child who keeps choosing it. Passion development is the mechanism. When the pursuit is internalized inside an autonomy-supportive home it becomes the child's own (harmonious passion) rather than something done to them (obsessive passion). I select families that can produce that, and I build the program to keep the child's assent live.

**Consensus it breaks:** the field treats an eight-year intensive path chosen by parents as something imposed on a child.
**Backing:** Ryan & Deci (2000); Vallerand et al. (2003); Bloom (1985). Honest limit: this holds only if passion development runs as harmonious, autonomy-supportive passion. Run as pressure it produces the burnout the critics warn about, so genuine consent is an execution requirement, not a given.

## DOK 3: Insights

- **Parents predict gains, not just levels.** Higher parental expectations predict how much a child improves, over and above prior achievement, and predict change more than prior achievement predicts expectations. That directionality is the license for ranking the family: it is a cause I can screen, not an echo of the child's score. (Pinquart & Ebeling; Hill & Tyson)
- **Heritability describes ordinary homes; environment bites where it varies.** Shared environment looks small only because twin samples span a narrow band of homes. In adoption out of deprivation and in low-resource homes it is large. The all-in home sits off that distribution, and what it buys is achievement under dose, not a raised adult IQ ceiling. (Polderman; Capron & Duyme; Turkheimer)
- **Screen the warm-demanding parent, and target conscientiousness, not grit.** The cold "tiger" profile underperforms warm-plus-demanding parenting on the child's own grades, and "grit" is mostly conscientiousness relabeled. So the admit signal is relentless commitment delivered warm, and the measurable trait already has a name. (Kim; Poropat; Credé)
- **Intensity harms through a specific channel, so I can design against it.** The damage in high-pressure homes runs through contingent self-worth and control, not through high expectations. A program that keeps expectations high while decoupling worth from performance attacks the mechanism. This is a design bet, not a proven safety result. (Luthar)
- **Unanimity screens the mechanism, not the demographic.** The two-parent advantage runs through resources and coordinated, low-conflict parenting, and a holdout adult is measurable conflict that predicts worse outcomes. Requiring every adult in the home to commit, at any household size, prices the thing that matters instead of the family structure. (Amato; Teubert & Pinquart; Buehler)
- **Binding commitment is both glue and filter.** People who bind their future selves follow through, and the ones who refuse to bind self-select out. The eight-year contract sustains the committed and screens out the rest in one move. (Bryan, Karlan & Nelson; Thaler & Benartzi)
- **Consent is ongoing assent, and harmonious passion is how an imposed goal becomes the child's own.** Self-Determination Theory shows external goals internalize into autonomous motivation under autonomy support, and harmonious passion is that outcome. So the stolen-childhood charge fails only when the program runs autonomy-supportive, which makes genuine consent an execution requirement. (Ryan & Deci; Vallerand)
- **Floors gate; commitment ranks.** The child's cognitive score and the family's ability to carry the eight-year cost are hard floors with no way around them. Everything above the floors is decided on family commitment.

## DOK 2: Knowledge Tree

### The family as the lever (SPOV 1)

**Pinquart & Ebeling (2020). Parental educational expectations and academic achievement: a meta-analysis. *Educational Psychology Review*, 32(2), 463–480.**
- DOK 1 facts:
  - 169 studies; parental expectations to achievement correlated r = 0.30 (cross-sectional), r = 0.28 (longitudinal), holding after SES controls.
  - Cross-lagged, expectations predicted change in achievement (r = 0.15) more than achievement predicted change in expectations (r = 0.09).
  - The link ran mainly through the child's own expectations, engagement, and self-concept, not homework policing.
- DOK 2 summary: The best evidence that a parent's expectations are not just a mirror of prior grades. After prior achievement is accounted for, higher expectations still predict improvement, and more than the reverse. That is the directional support "rank the family" needs.
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1007/s10648-019-09506-z

**Hill & Tyson (2009). Parental involvement in middle school: a meta-analytic assessment. *Developmental Psychology*, 45(3), 740–763.**
- DOK 1 facts:
  - 50 studies, grades 6–8.
  - "Academic socialization" (communicating expectations, values, and strategies) had the strongest tie to achievement, r = 0.39.
  - School-based involvement r = 0.19; home homework help was near zero to negative.
- DOK 2 summary: Not all involvement is equal. Transmitting expectations and strategy predicts achievement about twice as strongly as showing up, and homework help does not help. The parental trait worth screening is aspirational and strategic, not surveillance.
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1037/a0015362

**Fan & Chen (2001). Parental involvement and students' academic achievement: a meta-analysis. *Educational Psychology Review*, 13(1), 1–22.**
- DOK 1 facts:
  - 25 studies; overall involvement to achievement r ≈ 0.25.
  - Parental aspiration/expectation was the strongest component, r = 0.40; supervision the weakest, r ≈ 0.09.
- DOK 2 summary: The foundational synthesis showing expectations track achievement about four times as strongly as monitoring. It establishes the expectations-are-king pattern later, larger meta-analyses replicate.
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1023/A:1009048817385

### Why the twin-study consensus is misread (SPOV 2)

**Polderman et al. (2015). Meta-analysis of the heritability of human traits based on fifty years of twin studies. *Nature Genetics*, 47, 702–709.**
- DOK 1 facts:
  - 2,748 publications, 14,558,903 twin pairs, 17,804 traits.
  - Average heritability about 49%; the average shared-environment share is about 17%, not zero.
- DOK 2 summary: The largest twin synthesis to date. It bounds what ordinary parenting does across the homes in the samples, and the shared-environment share is small-but-nonzero, not absent. It cannot price a home no twin sample contained.
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1038/ng.3285

**Capron & Duyme (1989). Assessment of effects of socio-economic status on IQ in a full cross-fostering study. *Nature*, 340, 552–554.**
- DOK 1 facts:
  - French cross-fostering design crossing biological and adoptive-parent SES.
  - Being raised in a high-SES vs. low-SES adoptive home shifted childhood IQ by roughly 12 points, at both high and low biological backgrounds.
- DOK 2 summary: The cleanest evidence that rearing environment moves measured IQ by double digits when the environments genuinely differ. It shows the home lever is real, and it is largest across a wide environmental range, which is the range twin samples miss.
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1038/340552a0

**Turkheimer et al. (2003). Socioeconomic status modifies heritability of IQ in young children. *Psychological Science*, 14(6), 623–628.**
- DOK 1 facts:
  - Twins from the National Collaborative Perinatal Project, studied at age 7.
  - In impoverished families, shared environment accounted for most of the variance in IQ and heritability was near zero; in affluent families the pattern reversed.
- DOK 2 summary: Heritability is not a fixed constant. Where an environment has room to move a child, it does. This both sets the honest limit on SPOV 2 (the effect is clearest under deprivation) and supplies its mechanism (the all-in home creates headroom by removing what holds a child back).
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1046/j.0956-7976.2003.psci_1475.x

### Which parent to recruit, and the harm to design against (SPOV 3)

**Kim et al. (2013). Does "tiger parenting" exist? Parenting profiles of Chinese Americans and adolescent developmental outcomes. *Asian American Journal of Psychology*, 4(1), 7–18.**
- DOK 1 facts:
  - 444 Chinese American families across three waves over eight years.
  - Profiles: supportive (~45%), tiger (~28%), easygoing, harsh.
  - Supportive parenting produced the best outcomes; children of tiger parents had lower GPA and attainment and more depressive symptoms than children of supportive parents.
- DOK 2 summary: The study SPOV 3 must obey. Warm-and-demanding beat cold-controlling on the very outcome the program cares about. "Recruit the obsessive parent" only survives if obsession means committed and warm.
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1037/a0030612

**Poropat (2009). A meta-analysis of the five-factor model of personality and academic performance. *Psychological Bulletin*, 135(2), 322–338.**
- DOK 1 facts:
  - Cumulative N over 70,000.
  - Conscientiousness predicted academic performance at d = 0.46 (about r = 0.22), the strongest Big Five trait, independent of intelligence.
- DOK 2 summary: If a screen wants a validated dispositional target, it is conscientiousness. It predicts grades about as well as intelligence and independently of it, giving the "screen a trait" logic a solid home.
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1037/a0014996

**Credé, Tynan & Harms (2017). Much ado about grit: a meta-analytic synthesis. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 113(3), 492–511.**
- DOK 1 facts:
  - 88 samples, 66,807 people.
  - Grit correlated with conscientiousness at ρ = 0.84 and added almost no variance to GPA beyond it; only the perseverance-of-effort facet added incremental value.
- DOK 2 summary: "Grit" as a distinct super-predictor does not survive meta-analysis. Screen conscientiousness and perseverance, and do not sell a construct the evidence has deflated.
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1037/pspp0000102

**Luthar, Kumar & Zillmer (2020). High-achieving schools connote risks for adolescents. *American Psychologist*, 75(7), 983–995.**
- DOK 1 facts:
  - Excessive achievement pressure now sits among the top risk factors for adolescent mental health, alongside poverty and trauma.
  - The damage runs through self-worth made contingent on performance.
- DOK 2 summary: The harm objection and the "hazard" framing in one place. Intensity is genuinely dangerous when worth rides on results, which is the exact channel a program can design against while keeping expectations high.
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1037/amp0000556

### The household as the committed unit (SPOV 4)

**Teubert & Pinquart (2010). The association between coparenting and child adjustment: a meta-analysis. *Parenting: Science and Practice*, 10(4), 286–307.**
- DOK 1 facts:
  - 59 studies; coparenting cooperation, agreement, conflict, and triangulation predicted child adjustment.
  - The link held after controlling for individual parenting and marital quality, and predicted change over time.
- DOK 2 summary: How the home's adults coordinate, including how much they agree, carries its own weight beyond each parent's own parenting. It is the closest construct to "interparental agreement," and it supports requiring alignment among the household's adults.
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1080/15295192.2010.492040

**Buehler et al. (1997). Interparental conflict and youth problem behaviors: a meta-analysis. *Journal of Child and Family Studies*, 6(2), 233–247.**
- DOK 1 facts:
  - 68 studies and 348 statistical effects; youth ages 5 to 18.
  - Interparental conflict was linked to youth internalizing and externalizing problems at d = 0.32.
  - The size varied with SES, parental education, and which informant reported it.
- DOK 2 summary: Conflict between the adults in a home predicts worse child adjustment, at a small-to-moderate size. It is the basis for treating one dissenting or holdout adult as a measurable standing risk across eight years rather than a paperwork detail.
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1023/A:1025006909538

**Amato (2005). The impact of family formation change on the next generation. *The Future of Children*, 15(2), 75–96.**
- DOK 1 facts:
  - Add Health, over 17,000 adolescents; two-parent vs. single-parent gaps (repeated a grade 18.8% vs. 30.3%; suspended 21.2% vs. 39.8%).
  - Amato attributes the gap to resources, effective parenting, and cooperative coparenting, and calls the structure effect modest.
- DOK 2 summary: The canonical statement that the two-parent advantage runs through process, not the certificate. That is the basis for a unanimity-at-any-size bar: a committed single-parent home can clear it, and a conflicted two-parent home should not. He did not say structure is irrelevant, so cite the mechanism, not a headline.
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1353/foc.2005.0012

**Bryan, Karlan & Nelson (2010). Commitment devices. *Annual Review of Economics*, 2, 671–698.**
- DOK 1 facts:
  - Distinguishes hard commitments (real penalties) from soft (psychological) ones.
  - Reviews growing evidence that people demand commitment devices and that the devices change behavior.
- DOK 2 summary: The authoritative case that binding your future self works against self-control problems. A binding continuation contract is that device applied to a family.
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1146/annurev.economics.102308.124324

**Thaler & Benartzi (2004). Save More Tomorrow. *Journal of Political Economy*, 112(S1), S164–S187.**
- DOK 1 facts:
  - 78% of those offered joined; 80% of enrollees stayed through the fourth pay raise; saving rose from 3.5% to 13.6% over 40 months.
- DOK 2 summary: The strongest real-world evidence that a forward-dated binding commitment sustains follow-through over years, which is the mechanism the family contract bets on. Honest gap: 40 months is not eight years, so this is an extrapolation.
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1086/380085

### Consent, ownership, and passion (SPOV 5)

**Ryan & Deci (2000). Self-determination theory and the facilitation of intrinsic motivation, social development, and well-being. *American Psychologist*, 55(1), 68–78.**
- DOK 1 facts:
  - Autonomy, competence, and relatedness support drive motivation quality.
  - Organismic Integration Theory describes how external goals internalize along a continuum toward autonomous, integrated regulation.
- DOK 2 summary: The mechanism by which a goal introduced by a family becomes genuinely the child's own. It is the backbone of the claim that consent here can be real rather than imposed.
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1037/0003-066X.55.1.68

**Vallerand et al. (2003). Les passions de l'âme: on obsessive and harmonious passion. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 85(4), 756–767.**
- DOK 1 facts:
  - Introduces harmonious passion (autonomous internalization) and obsessive passion (controlled internalization).
  - Harmonious passion links to positive affect and well-being; obsessive passion to conflict and rigid persistence.
- DOK 2 summary: The distinction that decides SPOV 5. Passion development that runs autonomy-supportive produces the harmonious kind, where the child owns the pursuit. Run as pressure it produces the obsessive kind, which is the burnout critics fear.
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1037/0022-3514.85.4.756

**Bloom (1985). *Developing Talent in Young People*. Ballantine Books.**
- DOK 1 facts:
  - Retrospective study of 120 world-class performers across six fields.
  - The home and parents were central; early years were playful, with commitment deepening over time.
- DOK 2 summary: The classic account of how elite performers were made, with the family at the center and buy-in that grew rather than being imposed at once. It is qualitative and retrospective, so it motivates the model rather than proving an effect.
- Link: https://archive.org/details/developingtalent0000unse

### Ceilings, floors, and the limits of practice (context)

**Robertson, Smeets, Lubinski & Benbow (2010). Beyond the threshold hypothesis. *Current Directions in Psychological Science*, 19(6), 346–351.**
- DOK 1 facts:
  - Within the top 1% of ability, more ability still predicted more elite output, with no threshold.
- DOK 2 summary: Ability keeps paying off even among the gifted, which is why the child's score is a real floor worth setting. It supports "floor on the child, rank on the family" and warns against dismissing the score entirely.
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1177/0963721410391442

**Macnamara, Hambrick & Oswald (2014). Deliberate practice and performance: a meta-analysis. *Psychological Science*, 25(8), 1608–1618.**
- DOK 1 facts:
  - Deliberate practice explained about 26% of variance in games and about 4% in education.
- DOK 2 summary: Practice matters most in games like chess and least in academics, so a chess case cannot carry an academic claim, and hours alone cannot buy the target. The lever is the home that supplies dose plus conditions, not drill by itself.
- Link: https://doi.org/10.1177/0956797614535810
